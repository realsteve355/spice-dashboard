# MARS COLONY ECONOMY
## Token & Governance System

*The SPICE currency system — how it works, why it was designed this way, and what life looks like within it.*

---

## Founding Philosophy

The Mars Colony is built on one economic insight: in a world of sufficient automation, the cost of meeting every human being's basic needs approaches zero. Robots powered by solar energy can provide atmosphere, water, power, food, and shelter at negligible marginal cost. When that is true, the UBI becomes viable.

Every citizen receives a Universal Basic Income sufficient to cover all essential services, with a surplus for discretionary spending. That surplus — small at founding, growing as the economy expands — is the space in which human enterprise, creativity, and ambition operate.

Money is still needed. It drives efficiency, allocates scarce resources, and rewards initiative. But the purpose of the monetary system is not to determine who lives well. The monetary system determines how the surplus above the guaranteed floor is allocated and grown.

**The floor of basic social support is guaranteed for all. The opportunity ceiling is infinite.**

In a colony where robots provide for all basic needs, the purpose of work is not survival. Citizens do not need to work to live — the UBI guarantees that. Work is the means by which citizens contribute value and build wealth above the guaranteed floor. The mechanism for that wealth accumulation is ownership, not wages. Citizens earn above their UBI through equity stakes in companies — shares that vest with commitment, pay dividends, and appreciate as the company grows. The concept of employment in the conventional sense — selling time for money — does not exist in the SPICE system.

---

## Benefits of the SPICE System

| Benefit | Description |
|---|---|
| **Financial Security** | Universal basic income for life — covering all essential services with surplus for discretionary spending. |
| **Taxless** | Citizens pay only for services they actually consume, at metered rates. No income tax, no VAT, no mandatory contribution beyond the monthly MCC bill. |
| **No inflation** | The money supply is fixed. The monthly reset destroys all unspent S-tokens. Persistent monetary inflation is structurally impossible. |
| **Responsibility** | No free lunch — metered consumption of power, water, habitat, and waste processing incentivises efficient use of scarce resources. |
| **Entrepreneurship** | Long term savings and company equity provide the capital for enterprise above the UBI floor. The market is free and unprotected. |
| **Transparency** | All ownership — wallets, shares, assets, land rights — is publicly visible on the blockchain. No anonymous ownership. |

## Part 1 — The Token System

The colony uses five tokens, each serving a distinct and non-overlapping purpose. The full set is described below; S and V are the economic tokens that govern daily life, while G, O, A and L are identity, authority, and property tokens that make ownership and governance legible on the blockchain.

| Token | Purpose | Holders |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **S** | Spending currency — monthly UBI, all transactions | Citizens, companies, MCC |
| **V** | Long-term savings and company capital | Citizens, companies, MCC |
| **G** | Citizen identity and governance voting | Citizens only (one per adult) |
| **O** | Organisation identity and on-chain authority | Company secretaries and MCC chair (one per org) |
| **A** | Any economic claim — asset or liability | Citizens, companies, MCC |

The economic logic of S and V is described first because it governs the most visible aspect of colony life. G, O, and A are described in Sections 1.6–1.8.

### 1.1 Why Two Tokens Are Necessary

**S-tokens are for day-to-day spending.** Every citizen receives 1,000 S-tokens each month as their Universal Basic Income. They spend them on essential services, food, entertainment, goods — whatever they need and want. Any S-tokens not spent by month end are destroyed. This expiry is intentional: it keeps the money supply fixed at exactly 1,000 × citizens every month, which is what prevents inflation. S-tokens are not for saving — they are for spending.

**V-tokens are for long-term saving.** Citizens who want to build wealth above their UBI baseline convert up to 200 S-tokens per month into V-tokens. V-tokens never expire. They accumulate over a lifetime and can be redeemed back to S-tokens whenever the citizen needs to make a large purchase — a habitat upgrade, a surface vehicle, a stake in a company. V-tokens are personal savings made permanent.

Companies also need a way to carry value forward between months — to save for capital investment, to fund large projects, to accumulate the resources needed to buy equipment or expand. They do this through V-tokens too. Without a permanent savings mechanism, every company would be forced to live month-to-month and large enterprises would be impossible.

### 1.2 S-Tokens — The Spending Currency

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| Issued by | The Fisc (the colony's automated financial institution) — automatically, on the 1st of each month |
| Amount | 1,000 S-tokens per adult citizen per month (fixed) |
| Expiry | Midnight on the last day of the month — all unspent S-tokens are destroyed |
| Purpose | Pay MCC bills, buy goods and services, buy company shares, convert to V-tokens |
| Children | Receive full UBI from birth, managed by a registered guardian |

Children's MCC costs are real from day one — air, water, habitat, and medical are consumed equally regardless of age. The guardian spends the UBI on the child's behalf and converts up to 200 S-tokens/month to V-tokens. By adulthood a child has accumulated at least 43,200 V-tokens — a capital foundation that is entirely their own.

**Why this does not cause inflation**

Every month the Fisc prints 1,000 S-tokens per citizen. In a normal economy this would cause inflation. Here it cannot: every S-token that is not spent is destroyed at month end. The total money supply resets to zero every thirty days. You cannot have inflation without a growing money supply. This system has none.

Fractional reserve banking — the main mechanism by which conventional banks expand the money supply — is also impossible here. Only the Fisc can mint S-tokens. No private bank can create new ones by lending.

### 1.3 V-Tokens — Long Term Savings

V-tokens are the colony's permanent store of value. Citizens earn them by converting a portion of their monthly S-token UBI. They never expire and can be redeemed back to S-tokens at any time at full face value.

**Citizen rules:**

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Receive | UBI and service income in S-tokens · equity dividends in V-tokens |
| Spend | S-tokens only — all purchases and transactions |
| Save | Convert up to 200 S-tokens/month → V-tokens |
| Deploy savings | Redeem V-tokens → S-tokens 1:1, then spend |
| Expiry | Never — citizen V-tokens are permanent |

**Company rules:**

| Rule | Detail |
|---|---|
| Receive | S-tokens only — all revenue |
| Spend | S-tokens only — all payments |
| Save | Convert ALL net monthly S-tokens → V-tokens |
| Deploy capital | Redeem V-tokens → S-tokens 1:1, then spend |
| Pay dividends | Transfer V-tokens directly to equity holders' wallets |

**Why V-tokens do not cause inflation**

V-tokens can only be spent by redeeming them to S-tokens first. When redeemed they enter that month's S-token supply and are governed by the same monthly reset. Any V-tokens redeemed but not spent by month end are destroyed along with all other unspent S-tokens. The monthly reset is the final circuit breaker for all spending power in the economy.

### 1.4 The Monthly Token Flow

| Step | What happens |
|---|---|
| 1st of month | Fisc issues 1,000 S-tokens to every citizen wallet |
| During month | Citizens spend S-tokens on MCC services, goods, and company services |
| During month | Citizens may redeem V-tokens → S-tokens for large purchases |
| During month | Companies earn S-tokens, convert ALL net earnings → V-tokens, pay V-token dividends to equity holders |
| During month | Citizens convert up to 200 S-tokens → V-tokens as savings |
| Month end | All remaining S-tokens in every wallet are destroyed |

In the early post-founding colony, MCC services consume a larger share of the UBI — perhaps 50–60%. As the economy grows and competition emerges, prices adjust and the discretionary surplus expands.

### 1.6 G-Token — Citizen Governance

Every adult citizen holds exactly one G-token, issued on signing the founding constitution. It is soulbound — non-transferable, non-purchasable, non-inheritable. It is retired on registered death. Children do not hold G-tokens until they sign the constitution at adulthood (18).

The G-token is a governance instrument, not an economic one. It confers one vote in MCC board elections, recall referenda, and constitutional amendments. It does not confer commercial ownership of MCC or any claim on colony assets. Full G-token rules are set out in Part 5.2 and the constitutional protections in Part 8.

### 1.7 O-Token — Organisation Identity

The G-token and O-token are identity instruments, not economic ones. The G-token establishes citizen identity; the O-token establishes organisation identity. Neither pays dividends, accrues value, nor appears in a net worth calculation.

> **G-token is to citizen as O-token is to organisation.**

Every registered organisation in the colony holds exactly one O-token. This includes companies, the MCC, cooperatives, and civic associations. The O-token is held by the organisation's authorised representative — typically the company secretary or MCC chair — and transfers to their successor when the role changes hands.

The O-token is not a voting instrument. Organisations do not vote in colony governance. The secretary votes as a citizen, using their personal G-token. The O-token is an identity and authority token: it proves who speaks for the organisation on-chain, and authorises that person to sign transactions — dividend distributions, treasury operations, asset transfers — on the organisation's behalf.

The MCC holds one O-token, initially issued to the colony founder and transferable to any designated MCC chair. Having the MCC's authority held as a transferable on-chain token — rather than permanently locked to a founding private key — is a constitutional requirement.

### 1.8 A-Tokens — Economic Claims

> **Implementation status (April 2026):** AToken.sol is deployed on Base Sepolia as a full ERC-721 contract ("SPICE A-Token", symbol: ATOKE). All three forms described below are live on-chain. A-tokens are visible as NFTs in MetaMask and verifiable on Basescan. Public transfers are blocked — all transfers must go through the Fisc (Colony contract), which enforces the rules below.

An A-token is a Fisc-registered economic claim. Every significant ownership right and every financial obligation in the colony is recorded as an A-token. A claim can be positive (you own something or are owed something) or negative (you owe something). The Fisc is the sole issuer of A-tokens — citizens and companies cannot create them unilaterally.

**An A-token has four properties:**

| Property | Range |
|---|---|
| Sign | Positive (asset) or negative (liability) |
| Yield | Zero · variable (equity dividends) · fixed (contractual payments) |
| Counterparty | None (unilateral) or paired (bilateral agreement) |
| Value | Established at point of transfer; optional depreciation schedule for company-held physical assets |

**Three forms:**

**1. Unilateral asset token**
A physical object owned outright — a robot, a vehicle, equipment, a land parcel. One A-token, held by the owner, no counterparty. Yield is zero. Value is the last transfer price. A company-owned physical asset may carry a declared depreciation schedule registered at acquisition; the Fisc applies this to the book value used in accounting and net worth calculations.

**2. Paired equity tokens**
A company share. The Fisc creates two A-tokens simultaneously:
- An **asset A-token** held by the shareholder — records their stake in basis points and entitles the holder to a pro-rata share of the company's monthly V-token distribution
- A **liability A-token** held by the company — records the aggregate obligation to distribute to all equity holders

The company holds one liability A-token regardless of how many shareholders it has. At month end, the Fisc reads the company's liability A-token, calculates the distribution from net V-token earnings, and transfers V-tokens to each asset A-token holder automatically. No action is required from the company secretary.

When a shareholder sells part of their stake, their asset A-token is split: the sold portion becomes a new asset A-token for the buyer. The company's single liability A-token is unchanged.

**3. Paired fixed-obligation tokens**
A bilateral payment agreement — a hire-purchase arrangement, an advance against future earnings, a personal loan. The Fisc creates two A-tokens simultaneously:
- An **asset A-token** held by the recipient — entitles the holder to receive fixed periodic payments
- A **liability A-token** held by the obligor — records the obligation to pay on schedule

**Settlement priority:** The Fisc settles all liability A-token obligations before crediting monthly UBI. An obligation within the UBI amount therefore cannot be defaulted — payment is automatic before the citizen receives their net balance. The Fisc will not create an unsecured liability A-token if it would push the citizen's total monthly obligations beyond their guaranteed ability to pay (the UBI floor of 1,000 S per month). Structural default is impossible for any unsecured obligation the Fisc was willing to register.

**Secured obligations**
A fixed-obligation A-token may be secured by collateral. The borrower pledges one or more asset A-tokens at the time the obligation is created. The Fisc transfers the pledged tokens to an escrow register held under the Fisc's authority — the borrower cannot sell or transfer them while the obligation remains outstanding. On full settlement, the Fisc returns the collateral to the borrower. On default (a missed payment that cannot be deducted automatically), the Fisc transfers the collateral A-token directly to the creditor.

Secured obligations may be created for any amount, provided the pledged collateral covers the obligation's declared value. The UBI cap applies only to unsecured fixed-obligation A-tokens. A citizen or company may take on a secured obligation of any scale if they hold sufficient A-token collateral to pledge against it.

This is not fractional reserve banking. Paired A-tokens do not create new S or V tokens — they are commitments to redistribute tokens that will exist when payment falls due. The monthly reset operates identically regardless of what obligations exist.

**Net worth**

Net worth = V-tokens + S-tokens + Σ(positive A-token values) − Σ(liability A-token values)

A-token value is the last known transfer price, adjusted where a depreciation schedule applies.

**Inheritance**

Positive A-tokens transfer per standard inheritance rules along with V-tokens. Liability A-tokens transfer with the estate — outstanding obligations are discharged from inherited assets before distribution to beneficiaries. A citizen cannot bequeath more than they own.

**Land parcels**

Land parcels are unilateral A-tokens. In colonies where surface land is unowned at founding — Mars being the reference case — the Fisc operates as Harberger agent: the owner must declare a value, pays a monthly stewardship fee of 0.5% of declared value in V-tokens to the colony treasury, and any citizen may force-purchase at the declared price at any time. The owner cannot refuse. In colonies where land is already privately owned before the colony forms, land A-tokens transfer by mutual agreement like any other asset. Harberger rules are a property of the colony's founding conditions, specified in the founding constitution — not a property of the A-token type.

**Registration threshold**

Physical A-tokens are required above the registration threshold: declared value > 500 S-token equivalent, weight > 50 kg, or autonomous AI capability. Below the threshold, possession implies ownership with no on-chain record needed.

### 1.5 Billing on the Blockchain

MCC bills are not issued manually. Smart meters feed consumption data — power in kWh, water in litres, habitat in m² — directly to the Fisc smart contracts. At month end the Fisc calculates each citizen's bill automatically and deducts it from their wallet. Citizens can view their real-time consumption and projected bill on the blockchain at any moment. The bill is calculated and settled without any action required from the citizen.

## Part 3 — The Citizen

### 3.1 Rights

- 1,000 S-tokens issued unconditionally on the 1st of each month, for life — cannot be withheld, reduced, or means-tested
- One non-transferable G-token — one vote in MCC governance, issued on adulthood, retired on death
- Full property rights — all economic claims (A-tokens), including physical assets, land parcels, equity stakes, and bilateral obligations, are recorded on the Fisc blockchain
- Children receive UBI from birth. A guardian manages the wallet, covers MCC costs, and converts up to 200 S-tokens/month to V-tokens. By adulthood a child has accumulated at least 43,200 V-tokens — a capital foundation that is entirely their own from day one of adult life.

### 3.2 Obligations

- Pay the monthly MCC bill — itemised, variable, based on metered consumption
- No other obligations — no tax, no mandatory contribution, no minimum activity

### 3.3 How the UBI Evolves Over Time

| Phase | MCC bill | Basic essentials | Discretionary | Savings |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Post-founding | ~40% | ~30% | ~20% | ~10% |
| Early growth | ~30% | ~25% | ~30% | ~15% |
| Mature economy | ~20% | ~20% | ~40% | ~20% |

The floor is guaranteed. What changes over time is the ceiling — more to spend on, more to save for, more to build.

### 3.4 Two Ways to Earn Above the UBI

| | Equity dividends | Commerce income |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Monthly V-token distribution from company shares held | S-token payment for a specific good or service supplied |
| **Duration** | Permanent — shares accumulate; dividend recurs as long as shares are held | Expires at month end if unspent |
| **Who** | Company participants and investors | Anyone — sole traders, occasional services, goods sold |
| **Example** | A participant in the surface transport company receives 400 V this month from the declared dividend | A technician repairs a habitat seal for a company — 300 S paid on the day |

For ongoing participation in an enterprise, equity is the mechanism. For one-off exchange of goods or services, S-tokens are the mechanism. A **sole trader** is a citizen who provides goods or services in exchange for S-tokens, holds no company equity, and accumulates wealth by converting S-token surplus to V-tokens. The colony has no wages — no ongoing S-token payment for being a participant in a company.

## Part 4 — The Company

### 4.1 Formation

- Any citizen may found a company by registering it with the Fisc
- Registration is administrative — access to the payment system, not a licence to operate
- No exclusive territories, no state protection from competition
- Founding equity stakes agreed at formation and registered on the blockchain
- A company that cannot cover its costs fails — no subsidy, no bailout

### 4.2 Monthly Token Rules

**During the month:** earns S-tokens from customers, pays suppliers and sole-trader contractors in S-tokens, accumulates S balance. Does not pay wages — participants are equity holders, not employees.

**At month end:** converts ALL net S-tokens to V-tokens. The FD declares the monthly dividend — the declared V-token amount is moved to a distribution wallet and the Fisc distributes it pro-rata to all shareholders. The remainder is retained in the V reserve for capital investment. All remaining S-tokens expire.

### 4.3 Equity and Shares

Company equity is represented as A-tokens (see §1.8). All shares are fungible. They may be held, sold, or redeemed — but are subject to a vesting schedule when issued to participants.

**No wages**
Companies do not pay ongoing S-token compensation to participants. Participants receive equity. Their income above UBI comes from the dividends those shares pay and from the shares' appreciating NAV. One-off services or goods supplied to a company by sole traders or other parties are paid in S-tokens as a normal commerce transaction — this is trade, not wages.

**Vesting shares — participant equity**
When a company brings on an ongoing participant, the Fisc issues shares with a vesting schedule of 1–12 monthly tranches. The month-12 tranche is larger — a commitment bonus for completing the full year. Each month, one tranche unlocks. The holder may then:
- **Hold** — retain the shares, continue receiving dividends, next tranche approaching
- **Sell on the open market** — receive current market price
- **Sell back to the company** — receive current NAV (the company's V reserve × the holder's share fraction) in S-tokens, at the company's discretion

Unvested shares receive dividends monthly alongside vested shares — participants are compensated from day one. If a participant stops contributing, unvested shares are forfeited to the company for reallocation. Vested shares remain the participant's permanently.

**Open shares — investor equity**
Shares issued to outside investors or other companies are issued without a vesting schedule (or with a shorter lock-up agreed at issuance). Freely tradeable from issuance or vesting.

**Share value — NAV**
The floor value of any share is the current NAV:

> share value = company's V reserve × (shares held / total shares outstanding)

Market price may exceed NAV where buyers expect the V reserve to grow. The Fisc always honours sell-back requests at NAV, subject to the company holding sufficient S-tokens to fund the buyback.

**Dividends**
The FD declares a dividend each month. The declared V-token amount is moved to a distribution wallet; the Fisc distributes pro-rata to all shareholders (vested and unvested). The remainder stays in the V reserve. There is no obligation to distribute — the FD may retain all earnings for capital investment.

**Buybacks**
The company may repurchase shares from any holder at any time at current market value. Bought-back shares are cancelled, reducing total shares outstanding and increasing the NAV of remaining shares.

- Minimum share unit: 0.01%
- All shareholdings publicly visible on-chain — no anonymous ownership
- Shares may not be pledged as collateral for external obligations

## Part 5 — The Mars Colony Company (MCC)

### 5.1 What MCC Is

MCC is a company governed by the same token rules as every other company. It exists to provide essential infrastructure — the services every citizen depends on that cannot safely be left to market competition.

The board are the shareholders for the duration of their term. They own MCC commercially during their term and receive its profits as dividends. MCC equity is an **office-term instrument** — it does not accumulate permanently. When a board member's term ends (whether by election cycle, resignation, or recall), the Fisc automatically redeems their MCC shares at current NAV, paying in V-tokens. Incoming board members receive a fresh share issuance at the start of their term. A director who serves consecutive terms holds their shares throughout; redemption occurs only when they finally leave the board. Citizens do not own MCC in the commercial sense — they hold a **Governance Token (G-token)** that gives them democratic oversight without economic ownership.

MCC holds one **O-token** (Organisation Token), initially issued to the colony founder. The O-token is held by the designated MCC chair and transfers to their successor when the role changes. It authorises the holder to perform on-chain MCC operations — advancing the monthly epoch, updating service prices, distributing dividends — without requiring the founding private key. The O-token is a pure identity and authority token — it carries no equity and confers no economic rights. The MCC's institutional authority is therefore transferable and transparent, with no confusion between administrative control and commercial ownership.

| | |
|---|---|
| Board | MCC shareholders — own the company commercially, receive profits |
| Citizens | Each holds one G-token — governance rights only |
| Pricing | Commercial — the board sets prices to make a profit |
| Profit | Retained by the board; board may declare G-token dividends to citizens, subject to G-token holder approval |
| Mandate | Fixed in founding constitution — changeable only by 80% referendum |
| Constraint | May not compete commercially with private companies |

### 5.2 G-Tokens — Citizen Governance of MCC

Every adult citizen holds exactly one **G-token** — non-transferable, non-inheritable, issued on adulthood, retired on death. The G-token is purely a governance instrument. It confers:

- One vote in the annual MCC board election
- One vote in any recall referendum
- One vote in constitutional referenda requiring citizen approval
- The right to receive MCC dividends if the board proposes and G-token holders approve

The board sets their own compensation and decides whether to distribute any remaining profit as dividends to G-token holders. If the board retains too much profit, citizens vote them out. If they propose dividends, G-token holders vote to approve or reject them. The blockchain shows all figures in real time — revenue, costs, board compensation, and profit — readable by any citizen at any moment.

This is market accountability with democratic enforcement. The board behaves like any commercial management team, with the additional discipline that their customers can remove them annually and their dividend proposals require citizen approval.

### 5.3 What MCC Provides and Bills For

| Service | Billing basis |
|---|---|
| Atmospheric processing | Flat rate per month |
| Water | Per litre consumed |
| Power | Per kWh consumed |
| Habitat | Per m² occupied |
| Medical AI | Flat monthly subscription |
| Internal communications | Flat monthly fee |
| Waste processing | Per kg processed |
| Security and justice | From MCC general revenue |
| Company infrastructure levies | For companies using dome, power, water, comms |

### 5.4 The Board

| Role | Responsible for |
|---|---|
| Chief Executive | Strategy, constitutional compliance |
| Operations Director | Dome, life support, power, water, waste |
| Finance Director | Billing, accounts, Fisc liaison |
| Security Director | Physical safety, property rights, justice |
| Medical Director | AI diagnostics, health emergency response |
| Citizen Representative | Voice of non-entrepreneurial citizens |

Board members serve **one-year terms**, elected annually by G-token holders. Board compensation comes through MCC equity dividends paid during their term. When a term ends — whether by election cycle, resignation, or recall — the Fisc automatically redeems all outgoing directors' MCC shares at current NAV, paying in V-tokens. Incoming directors receive fresh issuance. No MCC equity accumulates with former directors.

The **automatic recall trigger** activates when the standard citizen MCC bill rises more than 20% above its 12-month rolling average in any single month. When triggered, the Fisc initiates a colony-wide recall referendum automatically — no petition required.

## Part 6 — The Fisc

The Fisc is the colony's fully automated blockchain financial institution. Not a company, not part of MCC. A constitutional utility — created by the founding charter, governed by rules not people, operated entirely by software. No board, no employees, no profit motive. It cannot be lobbied, bribed, or voted out.

**What the Fisc does:**

- Settles all liability A-token obligations before crediting monthly UBI (settlement priority)
- Issues 1,000 S-tokens to every citizen wallet on the 1st of each month
- Enforces month-end S-token expiry across all wallets simultaneously
- Processes S→V conversions (citizen cap: 200/month; companies: ALL net earnings)
- Processes V→S redemptions (uncapped, 1:1)
- Settles MCC bills automatically from smart meter data
- Operates the blockchain — authoritative record of all ownership
- Issues, transfers, and settles A-tokens — the sole creator of economic claim tokens
- Registers citizens, companies, assets, and share transfers
- Publishes all transaction data publicly — readable by any citizen
- Processes inheritance transfers on registered death

**Why the Fisc must be separate from MCC:** if the board that sets its own profit also controlled the institution that issues currency, the conflict of interest would be unacceptable. The Fisc serves all participants equally. No elected body has authority over its operation.

### 6.1 Cashflow and Commitment

Companies manage cashflow through their V-token reserve — redeeming V to S when expenditure precedes revenue within a month. This handles all timing mismatches between inflows and outflows without external instruments. Since companies no longer pay wages, the main intra-month outflows are supplier payments and sole-trader contractor fees — both ordinary S-token transactions settled as they occur.

Where one party must act before the other pays — a contractor delivers before the company settles — the bilateral A-token framework handles the commitment (see §1.8). A fixed-obligation A-token records the payment on-chain; the Fisc enforces it at the agreed date.

The forward purchase contracts, escrowed payments, and revenue-sharing instruments described in earlier versions of this document are superseded by this combination of V-token reserves and A-token bilateral commitments.

## Part 7 — Property Rights

### 7.1 The Blockchain as Legal Reality

All significant ownership is recorded on the Fisc blockchain. Ownership is enforced by cryptographic certainty. If it is not on the blockchain, it is not legally owned. A robot whose registered owner differs from the person operating it will not respond. A share transfer that does not appear on chain has not occurred.

### 7.2 The Four Registries

| Registry | Records |
|---|---|
| Wallets | S-token and V-token balances, full transaction history |
| Claims | All A-tokens — physical assets, land parcels, equity positions, bilateral obligations — with current holder, declared value, and transfer history |
| Escrow | Collateral A-tokens pledged against secured fixed-obligation agreements — held by the Fisc until the obligation is fully settled (returned to borrower) or defaulted (transferred to creditor) |

### 7.3 Physical Asset Ownership

- Registration required for assets above 500 S-tokens in value, above 50 kg, or with autonomous AI capability
- Below threshold: possession implies ownership
- Both parties sign transfers on chain
- Unregistered assets above threshold have no legal protection

### 7.4 Surface Land

Land parcels are A-tokens. They are owned, transferred, inherited, and valued using the same mechanisms as all other A-tokens.

In colonies where surface land is unowned at founding — Mars being the reference case — the founding constitution specifies Harberger stewardship rules, enforced by the Fisc:

- The owner declares a V-token value for their parcel
- A monthly stewardship fee of **0.5% of declared value** is paid in V-tokens to the colony treasury
- Any citizen or company may force-purchase the parcel at the declared price at any time — the owner cannot refuse
- The Fisc executes the transfer atomically: buyer pays declared value, ownership A-token transfers immediately

The self-correcting incentive: price too high → expensive monthly fee, idle land costs you. Price too low → someone buys immediately. Price honestly → fair fee, land held until someone has genuinely better use for it.

**First registration** on Mars is first-come-first-served: any citizen or company may claim an unregistered parcel by declaring a value and paying the first month's stewardship fee. From that moment the land A-token is issued and Harberger rules apply.

In Earth-based colonies where land is already privately owned before the colony forms, land A-tokens are issued at the colony's founding to reflect existing ownership. They transfer by mutual agreement at freely negotiated prices — no force-purchase mechanism applies unless the founding constitution explicitly adopts it.

## Part 8 — Constitutional Protections

The following require a blockchain referendum of 80% of all registered citizens to amend.

| # | Protection | Guarantees |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | UBI floor | UBI may not fall below 1,000 S-tokens per adult per month |
| 2 | UBI universality | UBI may not be conditional, means-tested, or withheld |
| 3 | G-token universality | Every adult citizen holds exactly one G-token |
| 4 | G-token non-transferability | G-tokens cannot be bought, sold, or inherited |
| 5 | MCC mandate | MCC may not compete commercially with private companies |

| 11 | Citizen A-token protection | Citizen positive A-tokens may not be confiscated by any authority, public or private. Liability A-tokens voluntarily created by the citizen are not protected. |
| 12 | Fractional reserve prohibition | Only the Fisc may mint S-tokens or V-tokens. No private entity may create new monetary tokens by any mechanism, including lending, debt issuance, or securitisation. |

Only the Fisc can mint S-tokens. Fractional reserve banking — the mechanism by which conventional banks expand the money supply by lending more than they hold — is therefore mechanically impossible. No private bank can create new S-tokens. This prohibition is now a formal constitutional protection (see row 12 above) requiring an 80% referendum to amend.

**Citizen V-token expiry:** Citizen V-tokens expire 100 years after their mint date. The Fisc tracks the mint date of every V-token batch. This is long enough that tokens saved from birth outlast any realistic lifespan, but prevents indefinite dynastic accumulation across multiple generations. At the 100-year mark, unexpired tokens are destroyed automatically.

**MCC G-token rights:** G-tokens confer governance rights over MCC — voting in board elections, recall referenda, and constitutional referenda — plus the right to receive MCC dividends if the board proposes and G-token holders approve. They do not confer automatic dividend rights or commercial ownership. Companies that grow larger than MCC are governed by their shareholders in the normal way.

## Part 10 — The Viability Framework

### 10.1 The Ergon — Unit of Productive Work

The **Ergon (Er)** is the colony's unit of productive work. One Ergon equals one hour of productive output from any source — human or robot. It is task-agnostic: a robot assembling components and an AI providing counselling both produce one Ergon per operating hour. The market — expressed in S-tokens — determines what that hour is worth. The Ergon simply measures the input.

| Worker | Ergons/hour | Hours/day | Ergons/day |
|---|---|---|---|
| Human | 1 Er | 8 | 8 Er |
| Founding-era robot (7.5× capacity) | 7.5 Er | 20 | 150 Er |
| Advanced robot (15× capacity) | 15 Er | 22 | 330 Er |

A single founding-era robot produces the equivalent of nearly 19 human working days of output — every day, without rest, without consuming a single S-token.

### 10.2 Why Robots Are Not Optional

The robot fleet is not part of the monetary system. It is the physical precondition for the monetary system to work.

### 10.3 The Viability Condition

The colony is viable when robot output is sufficient to run all essential services within the UBI, with surplus capacity for the discretionary economy and a buffer against bad months.

If the MCC bill plus basic food costs more than the UBI, citizens cannot maintain a decent standard of living on their income alone. The founding fleet must be large enough to prevent this.

For the post-founding colony of 66 citizens and 160 robots, this condition is met comfortably — essential services consume roughly 50–60% of UBI, leaving 40–50% for discretionary spending, savings, and enterprise.

As the colony grows and the economy deepens, essential costs fall as a share of UBI — not because prices are controlled, but because competition and efficiency reduce them naturally. By maturity, essential costs target around 20% of UBI.

### 10.4 Why Inflation Cannot Occur

Three simple reasons:

**1. Fixed money supply.** The Fisc issues exactly 1,000 × citizens per month. No more, ever. No government can instruct it to print more. No crisis justifies an exception.

**2. Monthly reset.** Every unspent S-token is destroyed at month end. The money supply resets to zero. Price pressure from one month cannot carry into the next.

**3. No money creation.** Only the Fisc mints S-tokens. No private bank can expand the supply. There is no debt instrument, no bond market, no quantitative easing. The money supply is what the Fisc issues — nothing more.

All prices are set by supply and demand within the fixed monthly S-token budget. If prices rise in one month, the reset removes the pressure at month end. Persistent inflation requires a growing money supply. This system has none.

### 10.5 The UBI Buys More Over Time

The 1,000 S-token UBI never changes in nominal terms. What changes is what it can buy.

In Year 1, a citizen's discretionary budget might buy food variety and basic entertainment. By Year 20, the same budget buys surface excursions, personal robots, robot hire, restaurants, education, art — goods and services that did not exist in Year 1, now at competitive prices. The UBI has not changed. The economy around it has.

### 10.6 How the Economy Grows

Three mechanisms drive expansion:

**Scope.** Citizens accumulate V-tokens and found new companies. Each new company creates a category of goods or services that did not previously exist, growing the economy wider.

**Capital compounding.** Companies reinvest retained V-tokens into robots and equipment. More capital produces more goods. The investment loop compounds.

**Frontier expansion.** The colony can replicate itself. A mine resolves a material bottleneck. A robot factory compounds Ergon supply geometrically. A second dome doubles capacity. Each expansion is a step-change in what the economy can produce.

## Part 11 — The Two Economies

### 11.1 The Essential Economy

MCC provides the services every citizen depends on — atmosphere, water, power, habitat, food baseline, medical. Prices are set commercially by the MCC board. Citizens hold G-tokens and vote the board out if they disapprove of pricing. The blockchain shows every citizen exactly what MCC charges and what profit it makes, in real time.

### 11.2 The Discretionary Economy

Private companies provide everything above survival — food variety, entertainment, goods, transport, communications, education, robot hire, and whatever else entrepreneurs identify as a market. No licences, no protection, no guaranteed customers. Companies compete on price and quality. Failure is possible. There is no bailout.

In the early colony the discretionary economy is thin — a handful of companies, limited choice, monopoly pricing in most categories. As the colony grows, competition emerges, prices fall toward cost, and new categories appear. The UBI buys a richer life each year.

### 11.3 The Two Kinds of Citizen

**Bob and his family** run a milkshake stand in the main plaza. Modest income above the UBI, small V-token savings, no grand ambitions. They attend the MCC election each year. They are content. The system asks nothing more of them.

**Zack and his consortium** identified the raw material bottleneck early, saved V-tokens for three years, filed a Harberger claim, raised capital from partners, and launched Hellas Minerals. His equity stake now pays dividends that dwarf his UBI. He is already planning the next move.

Neither is wrong. Neither is more deserving. The floor is identical. The ceiling is open.

## Part 12 — Frontier Stories

*Five accounts of citizens who chose the ceiling.*

### Story 1 — The Robot Factory (Year 12)

**Yuki Tanaka** arrived in Year 1 as a robotics engineer. She converted exactly 200 S-tokens to V-tokens every month without exception. By Year 10 she had 24,000 V-tokens and a clear view of the colony's binding constraint: not enough robot capacity for the private economy above what MCC needed for essentials. Nobody else had the V-tokens to solve it. Yuki barely did.

She registered Tanaka Robotics, redeemed 18,000 V-tokens, built three robots by hand in a rented habitat bay. The compounding economics took over. V-tokens from each robot funded the next. By Year 20, 300 robots, the most important company in the colony, and wealth that made her the richest person on Mars.

Political noise followed. A candidate ran on nationalisation. The MCC legal adviser ended the debate: the founding constitution prohibits confiscation of V-tokens and guarantees company freedom. Constitutionally illiterate. Dies in committee. Yuki does not attend. She is already working on the next problem.

### Story 2 — The Mine (Year 18)

**Marcus Webb** spent six years mapping the Hellas Basin. He found his deposit in Year 17 — iron, aluminium, and basalt, 4.2 kilometres from the dome. Filed a Harberger claim, built a consortium of eight citizens, raised 31,000 V-tokens, bought twelve mining robots from Tanaka Robotics. Hellas Minerals launched in Year 18.

Within two years the raw material constraint dissolved. Tanaka built robots faster. More companies formed. The discretionary economy widened. The bottleneck sequence worked exactly as designed — not by plan, but by the S-token price signal pointing entrepreneurs at the most profitable gap to close.

### Story 3 — Dome Two (Year 31)

**Amara Osei** had been thinking about Dome Two since Year 15. By Year 28 the geometry of Dome One expansion had reached its economic limit. She ran for the MCC board on a single platform. She won, delivered, and Dome Two opened in Year 40 — 800 construction robots, a decade of project management, one failed recall attempt. Initial capacity: 15,000 citizens. One colony, one Fisc, one monetary system.

### Story 4 — The Comms Satellite (Year 44)

**Priya Nair** could not get a reliable signal to Earth. Seven companies, 140,000 V-tokens, one areosynchronous relay satellite. Revenue extraordinary. Earth-Mars data traffic surged. The relay became the colony's most profitable single asset — and raised questions the founding constitution had not anticipated about inter-colony and off-planet economic exchange. Those questions remain open. Phase 2 will answer them.

### Story 5 — The Asteroid (Year 87)

By Year 87, the colony had 340,000 citizens and 2.8 million robots. The binding constraint was no longer Ergons, space, or bulk materials — it was rare element composition. Twenty-two companies pooled 3.9 million V-tokens into Mars Deep Resources. Eighteen zero-gravity robots. Asteroid 2031-MA7. The first return shipment: 14,000 tonnes of nickel-iron and platinum-group concentrates. Nickel prices fell 70% within two years. A new generation of robot components, previously impossible to manufacture on Mars, entered production.

**Tariq Al-Rashid**, who mapped the asteroid for eleven years before anyone would fund the mission, holds a 3.1% founder's stake. He spends most of his time in his observatory, tracking the next candidate.

### A Note on Those Who Chose the Floor

For every Yuki and Tariq there are thousands of citizens who received their 1,000 S-tokens each month, paid their MCC bill, spent their discretionary budget on food and entertainment and good coffee, raised children, pursued hobbies, and led lives they found meaningful and sufficient. The system required nothing more of them. The UBI was unconditional. The ceiling existed for those who wanted it. Most did not, and that was entirely their business.

## Part 13 — Citizenship: Registration and Lifecycle

### 13.1 Registration

Any person may become a citizen of the colony by signing the founding constitution on-chain. The act of signing is the commitment — a recorded, permanent acknowledgement that the citizen accepts the colony's rules and the authority of the Fisc.

On registration:
- The Fisc issues one G-token to the citizen's wallet
- UBI payments begin on the 1st of the following month
- The citizen's wallet is created and visible on the blockchain from that moment

There is no vetting committee, no approval process, and no fee. The constitution is open to all who choose to sign it. The only requirement is the act of commitment itself.

### 13.2 Children

Children are registered by a guardian at birth. The guardian — a parent or appointed adult citizen — signs the registration on behalf of the child and is recorded on the Fisc as the wallet manager.

From birth:
- The child's wallet receives full UBI on the 1st of each month
- The guardian manages the wallet, covering MCC costs and converting up to 200 S-tokens per month to V-tokens
- The child does not hold a G-token until adulthood

At 18:
- The citizen signs the founding constitution in their own right
- The Fisc issues their G-token
- The V-token pool accumulated by the guardian transfers entirely to the citizen's own wallet
- By this point, a child whose guardian converted the maximum each month holds at least 43,200 V-tokens — a capital foundation that is entirely their own from day one of adult life

### 13.3 Death

On registered death:
- The citizen's G-token is retired immediately — it cannot be inherited or transferred
- The citizen's remaining S-tokens are destroyed at month end along with all other unspent S-tokens
- The citizen's V-tokens and registered assets pass according to the default inheritance rules below
- The Fisc processes all transfers automatically on receipt of a registered death record

**Default inheritance rules:**
1. Registered partner receives all V-tokens and assets
2. If no registered partner: equal split among registered offspring
3. If no registered partner and no registered offspring: V-tokens and assets return to the Fisc pool

Citizens may register an alternative inheritance designation with the Fisc at any time. The most recently registered designation takes precedence.

### 13.4 Exit

Exit from the colony is not available in Phase 1.

On Mars, this reflects physical reality — departure is not possible. For earth-based colonies adopting the SPICE system, exit is a Phase 1 design constraint: a citizen who ceases to participate remains a registered citizen. Their UBI continues. Their MCC bill continues. Their V-tokens remain in their wallet.

V-tokens are colony-specific. They have no value outside the colony and cannot be transferred to another colony's token system. Phase 2 will address inter-colony mobility — the mechanism will be conversion via external settlement (BTC or equivalent), not direct V-token portability.

### 14.5 The Post-Founding Economy

At 66 citizens the Fisc issues 66,000 S-tokens per month. Almost everyone earns substantially above this through their essential role — a standard technical shift earns 300–500 S-tokens above UBI; senior roles earn more. The MCC pays in S-tokens for all essential work.

The discretionary economy is embryonic. Nineteen surplus robots and 66 people create a thin market. A handful of companies exist — someone producing better food variety from hydroponic surplus, someone offering recreation, someone making the surplus robot time productive. Bob's milkshake stand in its earliest form.

V-token savings accumulate slowly. The V-token pool is small, held mostly by senior technical roles. The market economy is simple by design. What is being built is operational capability, habits, and institutional memory — the foundation the mature colony inherits.

### 14.6 The Character of Post-Founding Life

Sixty-six people share a pressurised dome on a planet that offers no margin for error. They know each other — all of them. They eat together, work together, and cannot leave.

The nearest analogues — Antarctic winter-over stations, submarine crews, long space missions — share the confinement and interdependence but not the permanence. These people are not going home. This is home. The psychology of that permanence is the founding colony's deepest challenge, and why the mental health specialist is as essential as the atmospheric engineer.

Purpose sustains the founding colony. Every person there knows their work is immediately, irreplaceably important. These sixty-six people are doing something no human being has ever done. That knowledge, held collectively, is the colony's most important resource.

### 14.7 From Post-Founding to Mature

The transition happens gradually over roughly a decade. New arrivals come in skill-specific waves. The robot factory opens. The mine begins operating. The discretionary economy deepens. The MCC board becomes a genuine political institution. The Fisc processes thousands of transactions daily rather than dozens.

By Year 50, the post-founding colony is a memory. The mature colony of 1,000 citizens has the full S/V token economy operating as designed, constitutional protections tested and proven, and a private economy the founding generation could not have imagined from inside their dome.

The founding colonists built the base camp. Their successors built the civilisation.

## Part 15 — Deploying a Colony

The SPICE system is designed to be adopted by any self-defined community. A university campus, a housing cooperative, an intentional community, a neighbourhood — any group that agrees to run the system can deploy a colony. The Mars colony is the reference model. The mechanics are identical regardless of setting.

### 15.1 What a Colony Requires

Before Month 1, four things must be in place:

| Requirement | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Colony definition** | A name, a scope, and a founding constitution agreed by the founding group |
| **MCC designation** | A person or committee designated to provide essential shared services within the colony and bill for them in S-tokens |
| **Fisc deployment** | The smart contracts governing S-token issuance, V-token conversion, G-token management, and billing |
| **Citizen registration** | At least one founding citizen has signed the constitution on-chain |

### 15.2 The Launch Sequence

| Step | What happens |
|---|---|
| 1. Define scope | The founding group agrees what the colony covers — what goods and services will be denominated in S-tokens, who is eligible to register, and what the MCC will provide |
| 2. Designate the MCC | The founding MCC board is agreed — not elected, for the first year. They set initial service prices in S-tokens |
| 3. Deploy the Fisc | Smart contracts deployed to the blockchain. Colony ID registered. Constitution published on-chain |
| 4. Open registration | Citizens sign the constitution and receive G-tokens. Registration remains open — new citizens can join at any time |
| 5. Month 1 | The Fisc issues 1,000 S-tokens to every registered citizen wallet on the 1st. The colony economy is live |
| 6. First election | At the end of Year 1, G-token holders elect the MCC board for the first time |

### 15.3 The MCC in an Earth Colony

On Mars, the MCC provides life support — dome, atmosphere, water, power. In an earth-based colony, the MCC analog provides whatever shared infrastructure the colony depends on.

Examples:
- **University campus**: the MCC might be a student committee providing canteen access, printing, space hire, and event services billed in S-tokens
- **Housing cooperative**: the MCC provides utilities, maintenance, and shared facilities
- **Town pilot**: the MCC provides local services that merchants and residents agree to denominate in S-tokens

The MCC's mandate is set in the founding constitution and is changeable only by 80% referendum. The board is elected annually by G-token holders. All MCC revenue and expenditure is visible on the Fisc blockchain in real time.

### 15.4 S-Token Value in an Earth Colony

S-tokens have no purchasing power outside the colony. Their value comes entirely from what the MCC and participating merchants agree to accept.

The stronger the MCC's service offering in S-tokens, the more the UBI is worth — and the more compelling citizen registration becomes. A campus colony where the canteen, the library printing service, and a dozen student businesses accept S-tokens gives the UBI real practical value within its boundary. A colony where only one coffee stand accepts S-tokens gives the UBI almost none.

The founding group's most important early decision is what the MCC will provide and at what price. Everything else follows from that.

### 15.5 Phase 2 — External Trade

Phase 1 is closed-loop. S-tokens have no external value. The colony has no outside.

Phase 2 will introduce external settlement — a mechanism for colonies to trade with each other and with the wider world. The instrument will be BTC or equivalent cryptocurrency. S-tokens will remain internal to each colony. V-tokens will remain colony-specific and non-portable.

This section will be elaborated in a separate Phase 2 document.
