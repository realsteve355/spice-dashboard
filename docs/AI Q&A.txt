Question 3: Credibility of Thesis / Slow Burn Problem
You're right to flag this, and I think it's the most important strategic question.
The thesis is fundamentally sound. The timing assumption is wrong — and it matters.
The sovereign debt problem is real and likely terminal in its current form. The AI deflationary force is real and accelerating. The structural incompatibility between the two is genuine. I have no argument with the macro thesis.
But "a few months" for the resolution is almost certainly wrong, and history is unambiguous about this. Sovereign debt crises play out over years to decades. Japan has been running unsustainable debt levels for 30 years and has not collapsed. The Roman denarius was debased over approximately 300 years. Argentina has been in and out of crisis for 50 years. The 1970s inflation took approximately a decade to resolve. The UK's managed decline from reserve currency status took roughly 40 years from Bretton Woods to the ERM crisis.
Why does this matter for strategy?
It means three things:
First, the cost of carry on the short positions is a real drag on performance in the slow-burn scenario. Shorting JGBs has been called "the widowmaker trade" for 20 years — people have been right about the thesis and lost money repeatedly because the timeline was wrong. The short book must be entered progressively on indicator triggers, not positioned speculatively in advance.
Second, the PAXG base position at Green level makes perfect sense even if the crisis is 10 years away. Gold is not a short-duration bet — it compounds in purchasing power terms over multi-decade periods. The fund can afford to wait, and the Green allocation should be positioned to earn a return while waiting.
Third, the "Hemingway pattern" framing in the existing strategy document — gradually, then all at once — is actually the right mental model. Sovereign debt crises do tend to have a long slow deterioration phase followed by a sudden dislocation that happens far faster than anyone expected. The 2010–12 eurozone crisis looked slow for 18 months and then became acute in weeks. The 2022 UK gilt crisis went from "background concern" to "IMF territory" in approximately 10 days.
The recommended strategic response to a slow-burn scenario:
Structure the indicator system with hysteresis — positions are entered on breaches but not immediately reversed on small recoveries. Require indicators to return below threshold for 90 days before reducing positions. This prevents whipsawing in and out of short positions during the volatile "gradual" phase.
Weight the Green and Blue portfolio toward assets that earn a real return while waiting (AI infrastructure, commodity exposure) rather than being purely defensive. Pure gold at Green level is safe but earns no yield and creates investor patience problems over a multi-year accumulation period.
Be explicit in investor communications that the fund is a multi-year vehicle. The existing 8-year lifecycle in the financial model is exactly right. Investors who expect resolution in months will redeem at exactly the wrong time.
The slow-burn scenario is actually not unfavourable for SPICE — it gives the hard asset base time to appreciate, allows short positions to be entered cheaply before they become expensive, and means the fund can accumulate a substantial hard asset base before the acute phase hits. The risk is investor impatience, not thesis failure.
The scenario that would genuinely threaten the thesis is a politically successful resolution without crisis: if AI productivity gains were large enough, fast enough, to actually grow economies out of the debt burden without inflation. This would require real GDP growth of 4–5%+ sustained for a decade from AI productivity. It's possible — it's the "Scenario D" in your documents — but it would require policy choices (deregulation, technology adoption, structural reform) that current political systems show no evidence of making. Even in that scenario, the hard asset base in SPICE doesn't collapse — it just underperforms equities. The downside is constrained.
