**THE COLLISION**

*A Macro Thesis*

*Second Edition \| Updated with 2025 Data*

Two irresistible forces are on a collision course. Artificial
intelligence is delivering structural deflation --- destroying jobs,
compressing margins, concentrating the gains of productivity in the
hands of capital. Simultaneously, governments across the developed world
carry debt loads so large that their only politically viable exit is
sustained inflation. **Governments need inflation. AI is preventing
it.**

*The resolution of this contradiction will be the defining financial
event of the next decade. This document examines the evidence, models
the arithmetic, and maps the range of ways it might play out --- from
managed decline to systemic crisis. It also examines two wildcards that
could dramatically accelerate the timeline: the arrival of physical
robotics powered by AI, and the emergence of crypto as a public exit
from the fiat system itself.*

**Part One: The Debt Trap**

Before examining what is coming, it is necessary to understand where we
are. The sovereign debt positions of the major Western economies are not
merely large. They are, under any realistic set of assumptions,
unresolvable through conventional means. This is not a political
opinion. It is arithmetic.

**1.1 The Numbers --- and the Framework That Actually Matters**

The conventional framing of sovereign debt sustainability uses the
debt-to-GDP ratio against an arbitrary threshold --- most often the 60%
figure from the Maastricht Treaty, which was itself chosen as roughly
the average of member states at the time rather than derived from first
principles. The more analytically rigorous framework, formalised by
Olivier Blanchard in his 2019 AEA Presidential Address, examines the
relationship between two variables: r, the interest rate on government
debt, and g, the nominal growth rate of the economy.

The logic is straightforward. If g \> r --- if the economy grows faster
than the interest cost of the debt --- the debt ratio stabilises and
eventually falls, even without a primary surplus. Governments can, in
effect, grow their way out. This was the dominant academic view in the
era of near-zero interest rates. It was used to justify the debt
accumulation of the 2010s.

The problem is what happened next. After 2022, rates rose sharply and
the comfortable r \< g world ended. For most major sovereigns, r has now
caught up with or exceeded g, and the sustainability arithmetic that
underwrote a decade of borrowing has changed fundamentally.

The United States is the most important case. US debt held by the public
crossed 100% of GDP at the end of 2025 --- a level last seen only during
World War II. More significantly, the r vs g relationship that made this
manageable for a decade has now deteriorated to approximate parity. r ≈
g means the debt ratio no longer falls automatically. It stabilises at
best, and deteriorates with any primary deficit --- which the US is
running at nearly 6% of GDP.

Japan presents the apparent exception. Despite 255% debt-to-GDP,
Japan\'s interest cost appears manageable because the Bank of Japan has
spent thirty years suppressing yields through direct bond purchases. The
BOJ now owns approximately half of all outstanding Japanese government
bonds. This is not sustainability --- it is financial repression at the
most extreme scale ever attempted by a developed economy. The moment
yield curve management fails, a 3% rise in Japanese rates adds 7.65% of
GDP to annual interest costs overnight. Japan is not an exception to the
thesis. It is the thesis in its most extreme form, held together by the
world\'s most aggressive monetary intervention.

**1.3 The Four Pathways --- And Why Three Are Closed**

There are four ways a government can resolve an unsustainable debt
position. The evidence suggests that three of them are, for most major
sovereigns, either arithmetically impossible or politically unavailable.

**Pathway 1 --- Growth**

The r vs g framework clarifies precisely why growth is insufficient. For
growth to resolve the debt position, nominal GDP growth must materially
exceed the interest rate on debt and do so for an extended period. The
additional problem --- and this is the core of the thesis --- is that
the force that is preventing inflation is also suppressing nominal GDP
growth. AI compresses prices and wages simultaneously, which means
nominal GDP growth is suppressed even if real productivity improves.
Growth is not a viable primary pathway.

**Pathway 2 --- Taxation**

To bring the US primary deficit to zero through taxation alone would
require raising federal tax revenue by 31% --- from 17.5% of GDP to
approximately 23% of GDP. To run the 3% primary surplus needed to
meaningfully reduce the debt stock would require a 49% increase in the
federal tax take. For context, the US has not sustained a primary
surplus for more than a handful of years since the Clinton
administration. The political consensus required to raise taxes by
anything approaching this scale does not exist. The UK is more feasible
on the numbers but already has one of the highest tax burdens in its
history. Taxation is a partial contributor but cannot carry the load
alone.

**Pathway 3 --- Default**

Outright default is the nuclear option that no major developed economy
has chosen since the Second World War. A US Treasury default would
remove the risk-free asset from a global financial system built around
it. The dollar\'s reserve currency status would be fatally compromised.
The cascading effects through pension funds, money market funds, repo
markets, and derivative contracts would be systemic.

The more likely form of soft default is financial repression --- forcing
savers and pension funds to hold government bonds at below-market real
rates. This is already underway in various forms and is discussed as
Scenario B.

**Pathway 4 --- Inflation**

Inflation is the traditional method. It works by reducing the real value
of the debt stock. Governments throughout history have used this
mechanism, sometimes deliberately and sometimes as the unintended
consequence of money-printing. The post-WWII debt reduction in the UK
and US was achieved substantially through a combination of growth and
mild financial repression, with inflation playing a supporting role.

The problem --- and this is the core of the thesis --- is that the force
that would most conveniently resolve the debt trap is precisely the
force that the economy is fighting against. AI is delivering deflation.
Governments need inflation. The collision of these forces is not merely
an intellectual puzzle. It is a policy trap with no clean exit.

> *The pathway that arithmetic recommends --- inflation --- is the
> pathway that the most powerful technological force in history is
> closing off. Governments will attempt it anyway. The attempt will have
> consequences.*

**1.4 The UK Index-Linked Gilt Problem**

The United Kingdom has an additional structural constraint that makes
its inflation pathway more treacherous than the aggregate numbers
suggest. Approximately 25% of UK government debt is issued as
index-linked gilts --- bonds whose principal and interest payments are
tied to the Retail Price Index. This is the highest proportion of
index-linked debt of any major sovereign.

For the government, roughly a quarter of the debt stock does not benefit
from inflation erosion. If RPI runs at 5%, the real value of
index-linked gilts is preserved --- and the nominal payments increase
with inflation. The government is paying more, not less, on that portion
of its debt as inflation rises. The 2022 LDI crisis illustrated this in
its most acute form: UK pension funds holding index-linked gilts with
leverage faced catastrophic margin calls when yields spiked, forcing the
Bank of England to intervene as emergency buyer of last resort in the
same week it was publicly committed to quantitative tightening. It was a
preview of the dynamic the thesis predicts at scale.

**Part Two: The AI Deflation Wave**

The debt trap described in Part One has existed, in various forms, for a
decade. What is new --- what transforms an uncomfortable fiscal position
into a structural crisis --- is the arrival of a technological force of
deflation at precisely the moment governments most need its opposite.

**2.1 What AI Deflation Actually Means**

The word \'deflation\' carries connotations of 1930s bread lines and
bank runs. The AI deflation being described here is different in
character, though not necessarily in consequence. It is structural and
supply-side: AI reduces the cost of producing goods and services,
compresses the margins of businesses that compete with AI-enabled
rivals, and reduces the need for human labour in a widening range of
tasks. The deflationary mechanism works through three channels
simultaneously:

-   **Cost compression:** AI reduces the marginal cost of producing
    knowledge work --- legal research, financial analysis, software
    code, marketing content, customer service --- towards near-zero. A
    task that required a £50,000-a-year employee can be performed by an
    AI system costing pennies per query.

-   **Margin erosion:** In competitive markets, cost reductions flow to
    consumers through lower prices. The industries most exposed to AI
    automation face structural margin compression as AI-enabled
    competitors undercut on price.

-   **Wage suppression:** When a technology substitutes for labour
    rather than complementing it, the bargaining power of workers falls.
    Employers can offer lower wages because automation is available.
    This suppresses aggregate wage growth, which suppresses consumer
    spending, which suppresses inflation.

**2.4 The Jevons Paradox --- A Serious Objection, Honestly Addressed**

The most intellectually serious challenge to the AI deflation thesis
comes from an 1865 observation by the British economist William Stanley
Jevons, writing about coal. Jevons noticed that more efficient steam
engines did not reduce coal consumption --- they increased it, because
lower cost per unit unlocked new applications and new demand. His
conclusion: efficiency gains tend to expand total consumption, not
reduce it.

The legal services example illustrates this precisely. If AI reduces the
cost of legal work by 90%, does demand rise by 900%? In principle, yes
--- there is large unmet demand for legal services from people who
currently cannot afford lawyers. If access expands dramatically, total
legal activity could increase enormously even as the cost per unit
falls. Satya Nadella made the same argument about AI compute in 2025: as
AI gets cheaper, usage explodes, and total demand for chips, data
centres, and energy rises.

This is a real effect and the document would be intellectually dishonest
to ignore it. The Jevons Paradox is already visible in AI
infrastructure: inference costs fell approximately 900x between 2022 and
2025, and GPU demand and data centre construction accelerated rather
than decelerated. The cheaper AI became, the more of it was consumed.

However, the Jevons Paradox --- even where it fully applies --- does not
resolve the thesis. The reason is that it conflates two different
questions: what happens to total revenue in a sector, and what happens
to employment and wages. These can diverge dramatically.

Consider legal services under a Jevons scenario. A firm serves 10x more
clients at 10% of the current cost per case. Revenue holds. But the firm
employs the same number --- or fewer --- lawyers and paralegals, because
the AI is doing the work that previously required headcount. The Jevons
effect preserves or expands the revenue of the sector. It does not
preserve the jobs or the wages. And it is the wage and employment
channel --- not the revenue channel --- that drives consumer spending,
tax receipts, and ultimately the inflationary dynamic that governments
need.

There is also a crucial distinction between elastic and inelastic
demand. The Jevons Paradox occurs when demand is highly price-elastic
--- a large response to price reduction. For some AI-disrupted services
this holds. But for others --- financial back-office processing,
software documentation, compliance reporting --- demand is relatively
inelastic. Businesses do not consume more compliance reports because
they become cheaper to produce. In these cases, the price reduction
flows directly to margin compression and job losses without a
compensating demand expansion.

The agricultural analogy is instructive here. Farming productivity
improved by roughly 20x over the 20th century. Did demand for food rise
20x? No --- food demand is inelastic. The productivity gain flowed to
lower prices and, critically, to the elimination of agricultural
employment from 40% of the US workforce to under 2%. The sector still
exists and produces more than ever. It employs almost nobody.

> *The Jevons Paradox is real and should be taken seriously. But it
> preserves revenues, not jobs. And it is the jobs --- the wages --- the
> spending power of workers --- that governments need to generate the
> inflation that resolves their debt. An economy that produces more
> legal work with fewer lawyers is not one that generates inflationary
> pressure. It is one that generates productivity for capital and
> structural unemployment for labour.*

**2.5 New Industries, New Jobs, and the UBI Question**

The second major challenge to the employment thesis is historical: every
previous technological revolution generated predictions of mass
unemployment that did not materialise, because new industries emerged
that nobody predicted. Nobody in 1900 foresaw the automobile industry,
commercial aviation, Hollywood, or the internet economy. The standard
economist\'s response to AI displacement anxiety is that we are simply
failing to imagine what comes next.

This argument deserves to be taken seriously rather than dismissed. It
has been correct in every previous technological transition at the
civilisational level. New industries did emerge. Workers did find
employment. Real living standards did eventually rise.

The honest response to this argument has three parts.

First, speed. Previous transitions unfolded over decades, giving labour
markets time to adapt, retraining institutions time to respond, and new
industries time to grow alongside the disruption. The AI transition is
happening in years, not decades. GPT-3 to GPT-4 took two years. GPT-4 to
current frontier models took eighteen months. The feedback loop --- AI
helping to build better AI --- is already visible and tightening. Even
if new industries do eventually emerge and absorb displaced workers, the
transition period itself will be economically and politically brutal.
And the macro consequences --- the debt dynamics, the inflation
pressure, the bond market stress --- will arrive during the transition
period, before the resolution.

Second, breadth. Previous disruptions primarily affected physical and
routine tasks, and the new industries that absorbed displaced workers
were in the knowledge economy --- precisely the sector AI is now
targeting. The jobs that have historically served as the destination for
disrupted workers are themselves being disrupted simultaneously.

Third, and most honestly --- uncertainty. The document does not claim
certainty that new industries will not emerge. It argues that the
uncertainty itself, combined with the speed of disruption, is the macro
risk. Investors in previous eras had decades to observe whether new
industries were absorbing displaced workers before the fiscal and
monetary consequences of displacement became acute. This time the macro
consequences may arrive before the answer is known.

On Universal Basic Income: it is a serious policy response that several
governments are actively piloting, and it directly addresses the
\'workers don\'t get compensated\' problem. The difficulty is what it
reveals about the thesis rather than resolving it. Funding UBI at scale
requires either higher taxes (which faces the political constraints
documented in Section 1.3), more borrowing (which worsens the debt
position), or money printing (which is the inflationary pathway that
Part Three examines). UBI does not resolve the collision. It is, in
fact, one of the mechanisms by which governments attempting to manage AI
disruption through fiscal spending feed directly back into the debt
trap.

**2.6 Why This Transition Is Different --- Five Structural Factors**

-   **Speed:** Years, not decades. The retraining and redeployment of
    workers that softened previous transitions requires time that is not
    available.

-   **Breadth:** AI directly targets knowledge work --- the sector that
    has historically absorbed displaced workers from physical
    automation.

-   **Capital intensity:** Deploying AI requires servers and
    electricity, not factories and supply chains. The investment
    required to automate a task with AI is orders of magnitude lower,
    removing a natural brake on displacement pace.

-   **The Engels Pause problem:** During the Industrial Revolution, real
    wages stagnated for approximately 60 years before rising. The
    productivity gains went to capital for six decades. The internet era
    (1995-2005) showed the same pattern emerging again. AI accelerates
    this decoupling.

-   **No geographic cushion:** AI does not respect borders. Knowledge
    work that was offshored to lower-cost countries will be automated
    --- there is no further offshoring destination. The disruption is
    global and simultaneous.

**Part Two-A: The Wildcards --- What Could Make This Dramatically
Worse**

The base case for the AI deflation thesis is already substantial. But
two developments --- one technological, one monetary --- could transform
the thesis from \'significant macro stress\' to \'civilisational
inflection point\'. Both deserve explicit treatment as material tail
risks with non-trivial probability.

**W.1 The Robotics Singularity**

> *Wildcard: If AGI enables human-level physical robotics, the sectors
> currently insulated from AI disruption --- construction, agriculture,
> mining, skilled trades --- flip from LOW impact to extreme impact. The
> deflationary impulse would be of a completely different order of
> magnitude.*

The sector impact table in Part Two shows that roughly a third of the
economy currently carries LOW AI disruption risk --- the physical
sectors that require human dexterity, site-specific judgment, and
embodied presence. These are not minor backwaters. Construction is a
\$13 trillion global industry. Agriculture employs a billion people
worldwide. Skilled trades --- plumbing, electrical, HVAC --- are the
ballast of middle-class employment in most developed economies. The
reason these sectors currently show LOW impact is not that AI cannot
understand them intellectually. It is that controlling a physical body
in an unstructured environment remains extraordinarily difficult.

That constraint may not last. Physical robotics has been advancing on a
separate but converging track --- Boston Dynamics, Figure, Tesla
Optimus, and a dozen well-funded competitors are all targeting
human-level dexterity. The critical variable is whether AGI-level
reasoning can be combined with sufficiently capable robotic hardware to
produce systems that can perform physical tasks at human quality and
human speed. Several researchers place this transition within 5-10 years
under AGI scenarios. Others put it at 20-30 years. Nobody is confidently
placing it at \'never\'.

What makes the robotics wildcard genuinely alarming for the macro thesis
is the compounding effect. If software AI disrupts knowledge work and
physical robotics subsequently disrupts manual work, there is no
remaining category of employment that is structurally protected. The new
industries that have historically absorbed displaced workers --- which
were in the knowledge economy during the industrial transition, and in
services during the knowledge economy transition --- are themselves
simultaneously disrupted. The labour market absorption mechanism that
has resolved every previous technological transition would face its
first truly comprehensive test.

The deflationary implications of full robotics penetration into physical
sectors are staggering. The cost of constructing a building ---
currently dominated by labour --- could fall 70-80% over a decade. The
cost of food production. The cost of physical infrastructure. These are
the inputs to everything else in the economy. A simultaneous collapse in
the cost of physical goods and knowledge services would create a
deflationary impulse that no central bank has ever faced and for which
conventional monetary policy has no answer.

There is also a second-order effect specific to the AI-singularity
framing. AI is now participating in its own development. DeepMind,
OpenAI, Anthropic, and others are using AI systems to accelerate AI
research. The feedback loop is real and tightening. If that loop
produces recursive self-improvement at scale, the pace of capability
development could become superexponential for a period --- compressing a
10-year robotics timeline to 2-3 years. This is not a prediction. It is
an acknowledgment that the uncertainty range on AI timelines is
genuinely wide in the accelerating direction, and that the consequences
of being wrong in that direction are asymmetrically large.

The SPICE thesis does not require the robotics wildcard to play out. The
software AI deflation case against sovereign debt is already compelling.
But investors who want to understand the tail risk --- the scenario
where the collision is not merely a significant financial crisis but a
fundamental restructuring of the relationship between human labour and
economic output --- should understand that the current base case is a
floor, not a ceiling.

**W.2 Crypto and the Public Exit from Fiat**

> *Wildcard: Governments have managed previous monetary crises partly by
> controlling the alternatives available to the public. Bitcoin and
> crypto represent the first monetary exit in history that is genuinely
> censorship-resistant at global scale. If the public lose confidence in
> fiat faster than governments can manage, the mechanisms of past crises
> --- capital controls, confiscation, forced conversion --- may be
> materially less effective this time.*

Governments have managed monetary crises for centuries through a
combination of tools: currency controls, asset confiscation, forced
conversion, and the simple absence of viable alternatives. When
Argentina debased the peso repeatedly through the 20th century, the
public informally dollarised --- conducting transactions in USD, holding
savings offshore, building a parallel monetary system. The government
retained official control but lost effective monetary sovereignty over
significant portions of economic activity.

The crypto parallel is instructive and the differences are more
important than the similarities. Argentina\'s informal dollarisation
worked because dollars were relatively accessible and the US government
had no particular interest in preventing Argentinians from holding them.
The escape valve was provided by another sovereign\'s currency --- one
that was itself subject to the same systemic pressures the thesis
describes. When the global fiat system comes under stress, there is no
dollar to run to.

Bitcoin and crypto represent something qualitatively different. They are
not another sovereign\'s currency. They are not a claim on any
institution. They exist outside the settlement infrastructure that
governments control, cannot be frozen by any single jurisdiction in a
non-custodial structure, and --- critically --- are accessible to anyone
with an internet connection. The global simultaneous nature of the AI
deflation crisis means, as the thesis notes elsewhere, that there is no
geographic safe haven. But there is a structural safe haven: assets that
exist outside the fiat system entirely.

The specific mechanism by which public confidence in fiat could shift is
worth examining. It does not require a dramatic collapse or a single
triggering event. It requires only that a sufficient number of people
--- particularly the younger, more technologically literate generation
that has grown up with crypto as a natural habitat --- conclude that
holding monetary value in a system that governments are visibly debasing
is irrational. The shift from \'crypto is a speculation\' to \'crypto is
a store of value\' to \'crypto is where I hold savings\' is already
underway in parts of the world most exposed to fiat credibility stress.
El Salvador, Argentina, Nigeria, Turkey --- not coincidentally, the
countries most familiar with what happens when governments lose monetary
control.

The political economy here matters for the thesis in a specific way. If
crypto adoption becomes widespread enough to represent a meaningful
alternative monetary system, it constrains the government\'s ability to
use several of the mechanisms that have historically managed sovereign
debt crises. Financial repression --- forcing pension funds to hold
government bonds at below-market rates --- is harder to enforce when the
alternative is an asset that cannot be regulated out of existence in a
non-custodial form. Capital controls --- preventing citizens from moving
savings to better-managed jurisdictions --- are harder to enforce when
the asset moves via a cryptographic signature rather than a wire
transfer. The confiscation playbook --- as applied to gold in 1933 and
informally to foreign-currency holdings in various crises --- faces
genuine technical challenges when applied to properly managed
self-custodied crypto.

This does not mean governments are powerless. Punitive taxation,
exclusion from the formal payment system, mandatory KYC requirements,
and the simple fact that most people hold crypto through custodians
rather than in self-custody all give governments significant tools. But
the direction of travel matters. Each year that passes, the proportion
of the population that understands how to self-custody digital assets
increases. The proportion that views crypto as a natural monetary
habitat --- rather than a speculation --- increases. The infrastructure
that makes crypto practically usable as a store of value and medium of
exchange improves.

For the SPICE thesis, the crypto wildcard operates as both a risk and a
validation. As a validation: if governments do attempt aggressive
financial repression or capital controls, the population\'s exit option
into crypto may accelerate dramatically, which is precisely the scenario
the fund is designed to capture. As a risk: if governments successfully
suppress crypto adoption before the thesis plays out, the fund\'s
non-custodial structure faces more headwinds than anticipated. The
honest assessment is that governments have the tools to suppress crypto
adoption significantly but not completely, and that the more
aggressively they attempt it, the more they validate the thesis that
drove crypto adoption in the first place.

> *In every previous monetary crisis, governments managed public
> confidence partly by controlling the alternatives. For the first time
> in history, a generation facing monetary debasement has an alternative
> that no single government can fully control. Whether they choose to
> use it --- and at what scale --- may be the most important wildcard in
> the thesis.*

**Part Three: The Interplay --- What Each Force Does to the Other**

Understanding the collision requires more than observing that two
opposing forces exist. It requires examining the specific mechanisms
through which they interact --- because the interplay between AI
deflation and government-led inflation produces outcomes that neither
force would create alone. Some of these interactions are paradoxical.
Some are dangerous. All of them matter for understanding what the coming
decade will look like.

**3.1 Can You Have Both Simultaneously? The Ordered Scenario**

The most optimistic version of the collision resolution is worth
examining honestly, because it is the scenario governments are
implicitly hoping for. In this scenario: AI makes legal services,
software, and consumer goods dramatically cheaper. Governments print
money to keep nominal wages rising. Debtors --- including governments
--- benefit because they repay in devalued currency. The real value of
the debt falls. Workers see their purchasing power maintained because
cheaper AI-produced goods offset the inflation in nominal terms. The
result is an ordered, managed transition: wages up, some prices down,
debt eroded.

This is theoretically possible. It is also what economists call a
\'productivity dividend\' --- the gains from AI shared broadly rather
than captured entirely by capital. The question is whether the
conditions required for it to happen actually exist.

Three conditions must simultaneously hold. Workers displaced by AI must
find new employment at equivalent or higher wages --- in sufficient
numbers, within the timeframe of the debt crisis. The money printed must
reach wages rather than staying in the financial system as asset price
inflation --- reversing the pattern of 2009-2021. And the quantity of
money printed must be calibrated with sufficient precision to generate
3-5% inflation without triggering a self-reinforcing spiral. No central
bank has ever successfully executed all three simultaneously. The
Japanese experiment with this over thirty years produced, for most of
that period, the opposite of what was intended.

There is also a simpler version of your original question: regardless of
AI, if a government doubles the money supply year on year, would there
not be hyperinflation? The answer is yes --- and this illustrates the
control problem at the heart of the strategy. The government attempting
to print just enough to offset AI deflation while maintaining 3% overall
inflation is trying to thread an extraordinarily narrow needle in real
time, with imperfect data, against a deflationary force whose pace and
magnitude is itself uncertain. The governments that have attempted this
calibration historically --- and several have --- more often produce
runaway inflation than the ordered outcome. The Weimar Republic,
Zimbabwe, and Venezuela were all attempting to use monetary expansion to
manage an economic problem. The control problem proved unsolvable.

**3.2 The Transmission Problem: Why Printed Money Doesn\'t Reach
Prices**

The conventional assumption --- and the one that governments are
implicitly relying on --- is that if you print enough money, you get
inflation. Quantitative easing will eventually stimulate demand, raise
wages, and push up prices. The experience of the decade following 2008
demonstrated that this assumption is wrong in a world where the
deflationary force is structural rather than cyclical.

Between 2009 and 2019, the Federal Reserve expanded its balance sheet
from \$900 billion to \$4.5 trillion. The Bank of England, ECB, and Bank
of Japan pursued similar policies. The result was not the wage and price
inflation that conventional models predicted. Instead, asset prices
inflated --- equities, property, bonds --- while goods inflation and
wage growth remained subdued. The money was printed. It did not reach
the real economy in the form governments needed.

AI accelerates and deepens this transmission failure for a specific
reason: it attacks the supply side of the economy, not the demand side.
When AI reduces the cost of producing a legal document from £500 to £5,
no amount of demand stimulation reverses that price reduction. The
productivity gain is real. The deflationary effect is permanent. A
government can print money to increase nominal demand for legal services
--- but if supply has expanded by a factor of one hundred, the price
effect is zero or negative.

> *Governments can print money. They cannot print demand for labour that
> AI has made redundant. They cannot print away a cost curve that has
> moved permanently downward. The transmission mechanism that made
> inflation the solution to the debt trap is broken.*

**3.3 Where Inflation Does Appear --- And Why It Makes Things Worse**

The money that governments print does not disappear. It goes somewhere.
In the 2009-2019 cycle, it went into asset prices. In the post-2020
cycle, it went first into goods (supply chain disruption amplified the
monetary stimulus into real CPI) and then into services --- precisely
because services were the sector AI had not yet disrupted.

This creates a specific and politically toxic distribution of inflation.
Asset price inflation benefits owners of assets --- the wealthy. Goods
and services inflation hurts consumers --- primarily the middle and
working class. Wage inflation, which would make the overall picture more
bearable, is suppressed by AI\'s effect on labour markets.

-   **Asset owners:** Real estate, equities, and commodities inflate.
    The wealthy get wealthier in nominal and real terms.

-   **Middle earners:** Face goods and services inflation while wage
    growth is suppressed. Real purchasing power falls. This is already
    measurable in the data.

-   **Workers in AI-exposed sectors:** Face not just wage stagnation but
    structural unemployment. The jobs disappear, not just the wage
    growth.

-   **Holders of cash and bonds:** Quietly expropriated as the real
    value of fixed-income holdings erodes. The government\'s inflation
    solves the government\'s problem at the bondholder\'s expense.

**3.4 The Central Bank Trap**

Central banks are caught in a trap that has no clean exit. In a world of
AI-driven structural deflation, the central bank faces a specific
dilemma. If it raises rates to fight asset price inflation, it increases
the government\'s debt service costs --- worsening the fiscal position
and pushing the government toward more money-printing, which the central
bank must then accommodate or watch the sovereign face a funding crisis.
If it cuts rates and expands the balance sheet, it further inflates
asset prices, worsens inequality, and does nothing about structural
goods deflation.

There is a name for the condition in which a central bank loses its
independence and becomes subordinate to fiscal requirements: fiscal
dominance. Japan has been in this condition for thirty years. The Bank
of Japan has not meaningfully tightened policy since the 1990s because
the consequences --- a government interest bill that would dwarf tax
revenues --- are too severe. The question the thesis poses is: how long
before the Fed, the Bank of England, and the ECB are in the same
position?

**3.5 The Paradox at the Heart of the Collision**

Here is the mechanism that makes the collision so difficult to navigate.
Government attempts to generate inflation through money-printing have
the following effects:

-   Money-printing inflates asset prices. Gold, real estate, equities
    rise. This benefits asset owners and reduces their incentive to
    consume --- the wealth effect partially offsets the stimulus.

-   Money-printing increases nominal money supply. Some flows into
    goods. CPI rises --- but AI keeps reducing the cost of goods
    simultaneously, so the net effect on goods inflation is muted.

-   Rising nominal incomes (to the extent wages rise at all) are
    consumed by rising prices. Real wages stagnate or fall. Consumer
    confidence declines. Demand does not rise as expected.

-   The government, seeing insufficient inflation to erode the debt,
    prints more. Repeat.

-   Bond markets, watching this cycle and observing that the debt is not
    being resolved, begin to demand higher yields. Higher yields
    increase the government\'s interest costs. More borrowing and
    printing required.

-   AI, meanwhile, continues to deflate the cost of goods and services.
    The government\'s inflation problem is not resolved. Workers
    displaced by AI are not compensated. The tinder builds.

> *The government needs inflation to resolve its debt. AI prevents
> inflation in the real economy. The government prints money anyway. The
> money inflates assets, not wages. Workers face AI disruption without
> compensation. Bond markets price the fiscal deterioration. Interest
> costs rise. The government needs more inflation. The cycle continues
> --- until it doesn\'t.*

**Part Four: How It Plays Out --- Four Scenarios**

The thesis does not require a single prediction. It requires an honest
assessment of the range of ways this collision can resolve, and a
strategy that performs across that range. The following four scenarios
are not mutually exclusive --- elements of each are already visible, and
the sequence may move between them. What they share is that in all of
them, conventional fiat-denominated assets underperform hard assets and
crisis-resilient positions.

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------
  **A**   **Managed Inflation --- The Slow Burn**          *Most likely
                                                           initial phase*

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------

Governments succeed in generating sustained inflation of 4-7% for a
decade or more. Bond markets grumble but do not break. Central banks
allow real rates to remain negative --- nominal rates below the
inflation rate --- which slowly erodes the real value of the debt.
Financial repression is used to force pension funds and insurance
companies to hold government bonds at below-market real rates. Capital
controls are introduced incrementally, framed as financial stability
measures. The frog is in the water.

This is arguably the scenario already underway. UK and US real rates
have been negative or marginally positive for most of the past decade.
The erosion of bond holders\' real returns is happening. The political
packaging is that this is necessary to support growth and employment.
The human consequences are diffuse and slow: savings erode, pensions
struggle, real wages stagnate, housing becomes unaffordable for those
who don\'t already own. Social tension rises. Populist politics
advances. But there is no moment of crisis --- just a gradual decline in
the living standards of those who are not wealthy enough to hold hard
assets.

For the SPICE thesis, this is the base case for Phase 1. Gold
appreciates steadily. Commodity prices rise with nominal inflation. Bond
shorts carry a cost but compound slowly. The fund outperforms a
conventional portfolio over the period without a dramatic single event.

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------
  **B**   **The Government Fight --- Financial Repression  *Probable
          and Capital Controls**                           intermediate
                                                           phase*

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------

Scenario A\'s managed decline eventually bumps against market
discipline. Yield curve control --- the BOJ model --- is adopted by
Western central banks. Capital controls follow, arriving not as capital
controls but as \'macroprudential regulations,\' \'financial stability
measures,\' or \'investment requirements\' for pension funds. The
architecture of financial repression is being constructed now, in
peacetime, before it is needed at scale.

The crypto-specific risk is most acute in this scenario. A government
fighting to maintain control of its monetary system will not view
uncontrolled private money with equanimity. Punitive taxation of crypto
gains, mandatory KYC requirements, exclusion from payment systems ---
these are the tools available. Note that they are also the tools used
against gold: Roosevelt\'s 1933 Executive Order 6102 made it illegal for
Americans to own gold, forced conversion at \$20.67, then revalued to
\$35 --- a 41% confiscation by another name. The honest position is that
this risk cannot be eliminated --- it can only be managed through
jurisdiction, decentralisation, and the structural design of the fund.

The wildcard in this scenario is the public response. Previous financial
repression episodes --- Argentina, Turkey, Nigeria, Lebanon --- saw
populations informally exit the official monetary system through
dollarisation. The crypto parallel is that this time the exit is into an
asset that no sovereign controls. If crypto adoption is sufficiently
widespread when governments attempt aggressive financial repression, the
mechanisms of the past may be materially less effective. Governments
attempting to prevent citizens from holding crypto face a technically
different problem from those who prevented gold holding --- and the
population attempting to exit is more technologically capable than any
previous generation facing monetary debasement.

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------
  **C**   **Bond Market Dislocation --- The Sudden Phase** *Lower
                                                           probability,
                                                           highest
                                                           consequence*

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------

Every slow burn contains the seeds of a sudden crisis. Scenario C is the
moment when bond markets stop believing that the debt will be repaid in
real terms --- when the term premium demanded by investors reprices
violently. The Hemingway pattern: gradually, then all at once.

The triggering events vary. An Italian debt auction that fails. The Bank
of Japan losing yield curve control --- which very nearly happened in
2023. A US debt ceiling crisis that becomes real rather than theatrical.
The 2022 UK gilt crisis was a preview at small scale: in two weeks in
September-October 2022, thirty-year gilt yields rose from 3.5% to over
5%, pension funds faced catastrophic margin calls, and the Bank of
England was forced to reverse its quantitative tightening programme and
buy gilts as emergency intervention. If that crisis had been 50% larger,
the intervention might not have been sufficient.

A full Scenario C event would have consequences that are difficult to
overstate. Mortgage rates follow bond yields. Pension funds holding
long-duration bonds take catastrophic losses. Zombie corporates cannot
refinance. The banking system faces balance sheet stress. The housing
market freezes and falls.

> debt crises and military conflict is not coincidental. Governments
> facing existential domestic economic pressure have historically found
> external conflict a useful redirector of popular anger. This document
> does not predict war. It notes that any serious treatment of the risk
> landscape of a severe sovereign debt crisis must include it as a tail
> risk with historical precedent.*

          Case**                                           overall shape*

  ------- ------------------------------------------------ ------------------

The most honest assessment is that the collision does not play out
uniformly or simultaneously. It plays out differently in different
countries, at different times, with different triggering events. Some
sovereigns fail before others. The crisis is real but uneven --- and the
unevenness is itself important for the investment strategy.

Italy is the most likely first mover within the Eurozone. It has the
highest debt-to-GDP ratio of the major European economies, the slowest
growth, and the most fragile political system. Japan is the other
candidate for early dislocation --- BOJ fiscal dominance for thirty
years, inflation finally arriving in 2023-2024, and an impossible choice
between tightening (detonating the interest bill) and printing (letting
inflation run). The yen short thesis is the highest-conviction trade in
the active macro sleeve for exactly this reason.

In Scenario D, the portfolio\'s diversification across multiple
sovereigns, currencies, and asset classes means the fund captures
returns from whichever stress events occur first. Italy before France.
Japan before the US. Each crisis point generates returns in the relevant
positions while the hard asset base provides stability between events.

**Part Five: What It Feels Like --- The Human Consequences**

Macro theses are usually presented in the abstract language of
percentages and basis points. This section attempts something different:
an honest account of what these scenarios feel like to the people living
through them.

**5.1 Savings and Retirement**

In Scenario A, the visible consequence is the quiet destruction of
savings. A portfolio of cash and government bonds returning 4% nominal
in a 6% inflation environment loses 2% of real value every year. Over a
decade, that is 18% of real purchasing power gone. The people most
damaged are those who did everything right by the conventional rules:
they saved, they bought government bonds, they trusted that their
pension would be funded. The conventional rules no longer apply when
those bonds are being deliberately eroded.

**5.2 Housing**

In a managed inflation scenario, nominal house prices may hold or rise
--- but in real terms, house prices in the UK fell by 30% during the
1970s inflation even as nominal prices rose. Younger people, whose wages
are not rising with inflation, find houses no more affordable. The
wealth transfer from young to old, from tenant to owner, continues.

In a bond market dislocation scenario, the housing market faces a
different and more acute crisis. Mortgage rates track long-term bond
yields. If 30-year yields move from 4.5% to 7%, the monthly payment on
the median US home at current prices increases by approximately 35-40%.
Ireland experienced a 50% nominal house price fall between 2007 and
2012. The banking systems that held mortgage-backed assets faced
existential stress.

**5.3 Employment and the AI Displacement Amplifier**

Previous debt crises occurred against a backdrop of relatively stable
labour markets. Workers who lost jobs in construction or finance could,
over time, find employment elsewhere. In the AI displacement scenario
unfolding simultaneously with a macro crisis, the absorptive capacity of
the labour market is structurally reduced. The sectors that would
normally create new jobs --- professional services, financial services,
software, media --- are themselves being disrupted. There is no \'wait
for the recovery and then return to your previous career.\' The career
has been automated.

If the robotics wildcard plays out, this dynamic intensifies across
every sector simultaneously. The political consequences --- economically
stressed populations, structural unemployment, AI-driven job losses that
feel unfair and irreversible --- are already visible in embryonic form
in the current political environment. The thesis does not require this
to produce a revolution. It requires only that it produce the political
instability that disrupts financial markets and accelerates the
pressures already in motion.

**5.4 The Inequality Amplifier**

Every scenario in this document has one structural constant: the
outcomes are dramatically better for those who hold real assets, and
dramatically worse for those who hold cash, government bonds, or no
assets at all. Government-led inflation works by redistributing real
wealth from creditors to debtors --- and the largest debtor is the
government itself. The people whose savings are eroded are the
government\'s creditors. They are the people who bought bonds, held
cash, and trusted the system.

The people who are protected are those who hold gold, real estate,
commodities, equities in businesses with pricing power, and ---
increasingly --- crypto assets. SPICE is built on this structural
reality. It is not a bet on catastrophe. It is recognition that the
policy path governments must follow, whatever form it takes, involves a
sustained and deliberate transfer of real wealth from conventional
savers to holders of real assets.

**Part Six: What Rational Preparation Looks Like**

**6.1 Why Conventional Hedges Are Insufficient**

The conventional response to macro risk is to buy gold ETFs, Treasury
Inflation-Protected Securities, inflation-linked bonds, or defensive
equities. SPICE rejects this approach --- not because these assets are
wrong, but because the specific nature of this collision makes
conventional hedging structures inadequate.

TIPS and UK index-linked gilts protect against inflation as measured by
the official index. They do not protect against the scenario in which
the government changes the index methodology, caps the index, or
introduces capital controls that prevent redemption at market value.
These are not hypothetical risks --- index methodology changes and
financial repression have historical precedent. An instrument whose
protection depends on the goodwill of the entity creating the problem is
not genuine protection.

Gold ETFs hold gold --- but they hold it within the traditional
financial system, in custodians subject to seizure, reported to tax
authorities, accessible to capital controls. Roosevelt confiscated
privately held gold in 1933. The ETF structure did not exist, but if it
had, the order would have applied to it.

**6.2 Why the Crypto-Native Structure Is Not Ideology --- It Is Logic**

If the scenarios described in this document have any probability of
occurring --- and the evidence suggests they have substantial
probability --- then the infrastructure through which you hold your
crisis hedge matters as much as the assets themselves. A hedge that can
be frozen, confiscated, or rendered inaccessible by the entity creating
the crisis is not a hedge. It is a claim.

The crypto-native structure of SPICE --- deposits and redemptions in
Bitcoin or Ethereum, holdings entirely on-chain, no fiat rails at any
point --- is not a technical curiosity or an ideological preference. It
is the structural response to the observation that conventional
financial infrastructure is itself part of the system under stress. In a
capital control scenario, the fund cannot be frozen by any single
jurisdiction. In a confiscation scenario, the non-custodial on-chain
structure means there is no custodian to receive the seizure order.

The risks of this structure are real and acknowledged. Punitive
taxation, exclusion from payment systems, and regulatory pressure are
tools available to governments, and they would be used in Scenario B.
The fund\'s response is structural: Cayman Islands domicile, genuine
decentralisation, algorithmic decision-making that reduces the
\'identifiable controlling party\' problem, and geoblocking of the
jurisdictions most likely to act aggressively. These do not eliminate
the risk. They manage it to an acceptable level --- comparable to the
risk that a government confiscates physical gold, which has historical
precedent but is not a sufficient reason to hold no gold.

**6.4 The Honest Caveat**

No investment thesis is certain. The scenarios described here represent
a range of plausible outcomes --- not predictions. The macro thesis may
not play out on the expected timeline. AI\'s deflationary impact may be
slower than projected. Governments may find policy solutions that are
not currently visible. New industries may emerge that absorb displaced
workers more quickly than historical patterns suggest. The Jevons
Paradox may prove more powerful than the employment analysis implies.

The honest statement is that the cautious scenario --- where the thesis
plays out slowly and modestly --- still produces a fund return that is
competitive with conventional alternatives. The base and crisis cases
are where the fund earns its exceptional returns. And the wildcards ---
robotics, crypto adoption --- represent asymmetric upside that the base
case does not require but the fund is structurally positioned to
capture.

The fund is designed for investors who are not betting on catastrophe,
but who sense that something is structurally wrong with the global
financial system and want a measured, intelligent way to act on that
instinct. Not everything, but something. Not a panic response, but a
considered allocation. The zombie apocalypse framing is memorable ---
but the underlying proposition is simply that the people who prepared
during the gradual phase were better positioned when the sudden phase
arrived.

> *How did you go bankrupt? Two ways. Gradually, then all at once. ---
> Ernest Hemingway, The Sun Also Rises. The time to prepare is during
> the gradual phase. We are in it now.*
