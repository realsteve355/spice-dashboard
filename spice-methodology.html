Overview

The Core Thesis

The SPICE thesis rests on a single structural observation: governments with unsustainable debt loads have historically relied on inflation to reduce their real debt burden, because inflation raises nominal GDP faster than the debt stock, gradually eroding the debt-to-GDP ratio. This mechanism — sometimes called "financial repression" — was the primary tool used by the US, UK, and most developed economies to escape the debt burdens accumulated in World War II.[1,2]

What is different today is the emergence of a powerful countervailing force. Artificial intelligence and automation are structurally deflationary. When a software agent replaces a knowledge worker, the cost of that output collapses toward zero. Across millions of tasks, this creates persistent downward pressure on prices — precisely when governments need upward pressure.

The Collision

The SPICE thesis is that these two forces — the inflationary imperative of governments managing unsustainable debt, and the deflationary pressure of AI-driven productivity — are on a collision course. The resolution of this collision will define the macroeconomic environment of the late 2020s and 2030s.

SPICE is designed as a hedge against the most likely resolution: governments win, deploying monetary tools (QE, yield curve control, financial repression) to suppress yields and generate inflation, at the cost of currency debasement and a transfer of wealth from labour to capital and from savers to asset holders.

The simulation on Chart 3 allows you to explore the parameter space of this collision: how fast AI displaces workers, which policy tools governments choose, and what the resulting trajectory looks like across six key variables through 2035.

Model Scope & Limitations

This is a stylised macroeconomic model, not a full DSGE or computable general equilibrium system. It is designed to illustrate the directional logic of the thesis clearly, not to produce precise point forecasts. Several important limitations:

Limitations — Please Read

Single country. The model focuses on the United States fiscal position. Other advanced economies face similar dynamics with different timelines (Japan is further along; Europe varies by country).

No international feedback. Currency crises, capital flight, and geopolitical responses to US fiscal stress are not modelled.

Structural break at 300% Debt/GDP. Beyond this level, the model caps debt and assumes a crisis/restructuring event rather than continuing to compound. Real crises are more complex and context-dependent.

AI displacement is modelled as aggregate. Sectoral heterogeneity (some sectors displace fast, others slowly) is not captured.

Policy effects are calibrated judgementally against historical precedents and recent academic literature, not estimated econometrically.

Context

The US currently spends more on interest than on defence or Medicare. Under CBO's March 2025 baseline (which does not incorporate OBBBA or TCJA extension), debt held by the public reaches 118% of GDP by 2035 and 154% by 2054. With current-policy extensions, the NBER estimates 199% by 2054.[6] The CRFB notes these projections assume no wars, no recessions, and continued relatively low interest rates — all rosy assumptions.[7]

Core Debt Dynamics Formula

The model uses the standard debt accumulation identity from public finance. At each annual time step t:

Equation 1 — Debt Dynamics
D(t+1) = D(t) × (1 + r(t)) / (1 + g(t) + π(t)) + pd(t)
D(t)Debt-to-GDP ratio at time t (model starts at 1.23)
r(t)Effective nominal interest rate on government debt
g(t)Real GDP growth rate
π(t)Inflation rate (nominal GDP deflator)
pd(t)Primary deficit as share of GDP (spending minus revenue, excluding interest)
This is the standard debt sustainability identity. The denominator (1 + g + π) is nominal GDP growth; when this exceeds (1 + r), debt is self-stabilising even with a primary deficit. When r > g + π, debt compounds without bound.

The r > g Break Condition

The key insight from Reinhart and Rogoff[1] and more recently Blanchard[8] is that debt sustainability depends critically on whether the interest rate exceeds nominal growth. Historically, the US has benefited from r < g — the economy grew faster than interest costs, automatically stabilising debt ratios. This relationship began breaking down circa 2018 and is projected to remain unfavourable through the 2030s under CBO's baseline.[3]

Equation 2 — Sustainability Condition
Stable if: r(t) < g(t) + π(t) ← nominal GDP growth exceeds interest rate
Unstable if: r(t) > g(t) + π(t) ← debt compounds without bound
The CBO projects the average interest rate on US debt to exceed economic growth (r > g) by 2045 under current law. Under the SPICE scenario, AI-driven deflation (negative π) accelerates this to the late 2020s.

In the model, r is endogenous — it rises as the debt ratio increases, reflecting the empirical finding of Reinhart and Rogoff that markets begin demanding a premium on sovereign debt once debt-to-GDP exceeds approximately 90%.[1] Specifically:

Equation 3 — Endogenous Yield
r(t) = min(YCC_cap, (r₀ + max(0, (D(t) - 1.2) × 0.012 + pd(t) × 0.18)) × yM)
r₀Base rate (4.1% in 2026)
0.012Yield premium per 1pp of excess debt-to-GDP above 120%
0.18Yield sensitivity to primary deficit
YCC_cap4.5% if Yield Curve Control active; 14% otherwise
yMMonetary policy yield multiplier (0.58–1.00)

Productivity Dynamics

AI displacement has two simultaneous effects that operate with different timing: a productivity boost (the work still gets done, more cheaply) and a demand destruction effect (displaced workers earn less, reducing consumption). The model separates these explicitly.

Equation 4 — AI Productivity Boost
prod(t) = peakProd × ramp(t) × exp(-0.02 × max(0, t - rampYrs))

where peakProd = 0.02 + aiSpeed × 0.12
and   rampYrs = 1 + (1 - aiSpeed) × 4 ← slower adoption = slower ramp
Peak productivity boost ranges from 2% per year (CBO scenario) to 11.4% per year (Tsunami). These are consistent with the range of estimates in the literature: the Dallas Fed (2025) finds AI boosting productivity 3–8% annually in exposed sectors; IMF WP/2025/076 models 0.5–1.5% aggregate TFP annually.[9,10] The exponential decay after the ramp period reflects diminishing returns as easy tasks are automated first.
Equation 5 — Real GDP Growth
g(t) = max(-5%, 1.8% + prod(t) × 0.65 - demandDrag(t))

where demandDrag(t) = max(0, (unemp(t) - 7%) × 1.2)
The 0.65 pass-through from AI productivity to GDP reflects that not all productivity gains immediately translate to measured GDP (some appear as deflation, some as profit margin expansion not captured in output). The demand drag term captures the Keynesian shortfall from mass unemployment: each percentage point of unemployment above 7% reduces GDP growth by 1.2pp. This is consistent with Okun's Law calibration for the US economy.

The Displacement Lag

A crucial and often-overlooked feature of technological displacement is the lag between productivity improvement and employment destruction. Firms adopt AI tools before they restructure their workforce. In the model, displacement lags productivity by approximately two years:

Equation 6 — Annual Displacement Rate
annDisp(t) = peakDisp × dRamp(t) × exp(-0.03 × max(0, dLag - rampYrs))

where dLag = max(0, t - 2) ← 2-year lag vs productivity
and   peakDisp = dispRate × 0.28 ← annual peak displacement fraction
The two-year lag is motivated by historical analogies: during the PC revolution of the 1980s and the internet era of the 1990s, productivity accelerated 2–4 years before the corresponding employment restructuring. The lag creates the "ghost GDP" phenomenon described below.

The Ghost GDP Phenomenon

Key Concept — Ghost GDP

Ghost GDP describes the period during which AI-driven productivity shows up in output statistics (GDP keeps growing, corporate profits rise) but has not yet caused the corresponding unemployment wave. The economy looks healthy on headline numbers, masking the building displacement pressure. This is the window during which the fiscal situation deteriorates most deceptively — tax revenues are still strong, so the debt trajectory looks manageable.

In the model, Ghost GDP onset is flagged when productivity > 3% AND unemployment > 8% simultaneously — the moment the divergence becomes unmistakable.

Chapter 3

The Collision

The "collision" is the meeting of the debt trajectory (which requires inflation to stabilise) and AI-driven deflation (which makes that inflation harder to generate). The model captures this through three interacting channels.

The Fiscal Channel

AI displacement directly damages the fiscal position through two mechanisms: tax revenue collapse (fewer employed workers paying income and payroll taxes) and welfare spending surge (more unemployed workers claiming benefits).

Equation 7 — Tax Revenue
tax(t) = tax₀ × (employed(t) / fullEmployment)1.2 + robotTaxBonus
Base tax rate 18% of GDP. The 1.2 exponent reflects that unemployment disproportionately hits lower-income workers who have higher marginal propensity to consume taxable goods. Robot Tax (if active) adds 0.8% of GDP in revenue from automation levies.
Equation 8 — Government Spending
spending(t) = base + max(0, (unemp(t) - 5%) × 2.2) + UBI_cost
base24.5% of GDP (baseline federal spending)
2.2Automatic stabiliser multiplier: each 1pp of unemployment above 5% adds 2.2pp to spending/GDP
UBI_cost2.2% of GDP if Universal Basic Income active (Robot Tax + UBI policy)

Inflation Dynamics

Inflation in the model is driven by the net of three forces: a baseline (which decays over time as the current inflationary episode fades), a deflationary drag from AI productivity, and a monetary policy additive from stimulus.

Equation 9 — Inflation Rate
π(t) = max(-10%, min(20%, base_infl(t) - prod(t) × 0.5 + iA))

where base_infl(t) = max(0, 2.5% - t × 0.07%)
The 0.5 pass-through from productivity to deflation is conservative — some AI productivity gains appear as quality improvements (hedonic deflation) captured partially in price indices, and some appear as profit margin expansion rather than price reductions. The iA term is the inflation additive from monetary policy (e.g. +3.5% for QE, +4.5% for YCC).

Break Point Logic

The simulation flags a fiscal break point — the year in which the system crosses a threshold suggesting a major disruptive event (sovereign debt crisis, hyperinflationary episode, or political rupture). This is triggered by any of:

TriggerThresholdEconomic rationale
Debt/GDP > 175% Reinhart-Rogoff "danger zone"; above this, historical default/restructuring rates increase sharply[1]
Unemployment > 20% Depression-level unemployment; historical precedent for political rupture and fiscal emergency
Deflation < −7% Fisher debt-deflation spiral: falling prices increase real debt burden, creating self-reinforcing crisis[11]
Yield + Debt yield > 6.5% AND debt > 150% Interest service becomes unmanageable; Italy/Greece 2010–12 analogue — markets withdraw funding
Note on Break Point Modelling

The break point is a signal, not a forecast of a specific event. Real sovereign crises are complex, path-dependent, and often delayed by political will and international support. The model does not attempt to forecast which type of crisis occurs, only to flag that the system has entered a regime where historical precedent suggests major disruption becomes likely.

Chapter 4

Policy Responses

The simulation models fiscal and monetary policy responses as independent, combinable choices. A government can simultaneously run an austerity programme and deploy yield curve control — indeed, several historical examples (Japan 2013–2023, UK 1940s–1950s) show exactly this combination. The effects are combined: multipliers multiply, additive terms sum.

Equation 10 — Combined Policy Effects
e_combined = {
  dM = fiscal.dM ← deficit multiplier (fiscal only)
  yM = monetary.yM ← yield multiplier (monetary only)
  iA = fiscal.iA + monetary.iA ← inflation additives sum
  gM = monetary.gM ← gold/crypto multiplier (monetary only)
  lD = fiscal.lD + monetary.lD ← labour share delta sums
}

Fiscal Policy Options

None (baseline)

No fiscal adjustment. Government continues on current trajectory. Automatic stabilisers operate (welfare rises with unemployment) but no deliberate policy change.

Deficit mult: 1.00 (unchanged)
Inflation add: 0.0%
Labour share effect: neutral
Robot Tax + UBI

A levy on automated production units funds universal basic income transfers. The only credible fiscal response pairing — UBI without a funding mechanism is not modelled as it is fiscally incoherent.

Deficit mult: 0.72 (revenue from robot tax offsets UBI cost partially)
Inflation add: +0.2% (UBI increases demand slightly)
Labour share effect: +8pp (redistribution compresses K-gap)
Capital share effect: −6pp (robot tax reduces post-tax profit share)
Austerity

Discretionary spending cuts capped at 92% of baseline spending. Historically: reduces deficit in the short run, but suppresses demand and worsens unemployment, partially offsetting fiscal gains.

Deficit mult: 0.60 (spending cut)
Inflation add: −1.0% (demand suppression is deflationary)
Unemployment mult: 1.15 (austerity worsens displacement)
Note: deflationary effect worsens real debt burden

Monetary Policy Options

QE / Money Printing

Central bank purchases government bonds, suppressing yields and monetising deficit. Historical precedents: US/UK 2009–2021, Japan ongoing. Primary mechanism for SPICE hedge thesis.

Yield mult: 0.70 (suppresses yields 30%)
Inflation add: +3.5% (monetary expansion is inflationary)
Gold/crypto mult: 1.55 (monetary debasement drives hard asset demand)
Labour share: −5pp (asset price inflation favours capital holders)
Yield Curve Control

Central bank explicitly caps long-end yield at 4.5% (Japan BOJ 2016–2023 playbook). Requires unlimited bond purchases to defend the cap; inherently inflationary over time.

Yield hard cap: 4.5%
Inflation add: +4.5% (most inflationary option — forces negative real rates)
Gold/crypto mult: 1.70 (most bullish for hard assets)
Historical precedent: Japan (yen −40%), UK 1940s–50s, US 1942–1951
Financial Repression

Regulatory requirements force banks, pension funds, and insurance companies to hold government bonds at below-market rates. A "silent tax" on savers and institutional investors. Historically the dominant mechanism for post-WWII debt reduction.[2]

Yield mult: 0.72 (regulatory suppression of market-clearing yields)
Inflation add: +2.0% (moderate inflationary effect)
Gold/crypto mult: 1.35 (moderate debasement signal)
Historical precedent: US/UK 1945–1980 (real rates −3% to −5% average)

Chapter 5

K-Shaped Economy

The K-shape panel in the simulation tracks the distribution of national income between labour and capital — arguably the most consequential long-run variable, and the one least visible in headline macroeconomic statistics.

Income Share Accounting

Why Labour + Capital ≠ 100%

In National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA) methodology, GDP is divided between: Labour compensation (~60% historically), Capital income (corporate profits + proprietors' income, ~25%), and a residual (~15%) comprising depreciation allowances, net taxes on production, and net interest. The K-shape chart shows the transfer within the ~85% labour+capital share. The residual is assumed stable. This decomposition follows BEA methodology.[12]

Equation 11 — K-Shape Dynamics
labShare(t+1) = max(30%, labShare(t) - kShift(t) + lD × 0.025)
capShare(t+1) = min(52%, capShare(t) + kShift(t) × 0.55 + cD × 0.025)

where kShift(t) = annDisp(t) × 0.45
kShiftAnnual transfer from labour to capital share, driven by displacement rate
0.55Capital captures 55% of the transfer (45% "disappears" into the residual / depreciation of human capital)
lD, cDPolicy-driven adjustments to labour and capital shares respectively
30% floorLabour share floor — even in extreme scenarios, some human labour remains economically necessary
Starting values: labour share 60%, capital share 25% — consistent with US BEA data for 2023–2025.[12] The long-run decline in US labour share from ~65% in 1960 to ~57% in 2020 (pre-pandemic) is well-documented and consistent with the direction of this model's dynamics even before AI acceleration.[13]

Policy Effects on Income Distribution

The K-shape is where policy choice makes the most direct distributional difference:

PolicyLabour Share EffectCapital Share EffectMechanism
Robot Tax + UBI +8pp (compresses gap) −6pp Redistribution from automation profits to workers via UBI; robot tax reduces post-tax capital returns
QE −5pp (widens gap) +7pp Asset price inflation primarily benefits capital owners; wage inflation lags financial asset returns
YCC −6pp +8pp Most extreme: forces savers into negative real rates while asset owners benefit from suppressed borrowing costs
Austerity −2pp +1pp Spending cuts disproportionately reduce public sector wages and transfers to lower-income households
Financial Repression −3pp +4pp Suppressed deposit rates and forced institutional buying reduce returns on household savings, transfer to government/corporates
Key Insight

The policy responses most likely to be deployed (QE, YCC, financial repression) are precisely those that widen the K-shape the most. This is not a coincidence — these policies are attractive to governments partly because they distribute pain silently (via inflation and financial repression) rather than visibly (via tax increases or austerity). The political economy strongly favours monetary over fiscal adjustment.

This is why SPICE invests in assets that benefit from the monetary responses: gold and crypto are direct beneficiaries of exactly the policies that governments are most likely to choose.

Chapter 6

Gold & Crypto as Hedge

The Gold/Crypto index in the simulation represents the combined performance of hard monetary assets — gold and Bitcoin/crypto — as a function of monetary stress. This is the channel through which SPICE captures returns.

Monetary Stress Index

Equation 12 — Monetary Stress Index
mStress(t) = max(0, r(t) - 4%) × 4 ← yield above neutral (fiscal stress)
            + max(0, -π(t)) × 3 ← deflation (debt spiral risk)
            + max(0, π(t) - 4%) × 4 ← excess inflation (debasement signal)
            + max(0, D(t) - 140%) × 0.8 ← high debt overhang
The index grows at: Gold(t+1) = Gold(t) × (1 + min(mStress × 0.12 × gM, 60%) + 3%). The 3% base growth reflects gold's long-run real return and Bitcoin's network growth premium. The 60% annual cap prevents unrealistic single-year returns.

The Hedge Rationale

The four components of monetary stress represent the four distinct paths through which the debt-AI collision resolves badly for fiat money:

ComponentWhat it measuresGold/BTC historical responseKey source
Yield above 4% Fiscal credibility stress; markets demanding risk premium Gold +15–25% in high-yield stress episodes (2008, 2022)[14] World Gold Council[14]
Deflation Debt-deflation spiral risk; real debt burden rising Gold +15% in deflationary episodes; BTC untested at scale Reinhart-Rogoff[1]
Inflation > 4% Currency debasement; monetary expansion beyond target Gold +8–40% in high-inflation regimes; BTC design as inflation hedge[15,16] WisdomTree; Grayscale[15,16]
Debt > 140% GDP Structural insolvency risk; sovereign credibility eroding Gold +30–50% in sovereign stress (Greece 2010–12, Argentina multiple)[14] World Gold Council; NYDIG[17]
Why Both Gold and Crypto?

Gold and Bitcoin are complementary hedges with different risk profiles. Gold has 5,000 years of monetary history, is held by central banks, and performs well in deflation as well as inflation — because its value is not a claim on any cash flow, it cannot be impaired by deflation the way equities or bonds can. Bitcoin is a newer, more volatile asset with a hard supply cap and network effects; it is more sensitive to institutional adoption and regulatory risk, but offers higher upside in a monetary reset scenario. The SPICE index weights both, capturing the institutional-grade safety of gold with the optionality of crypto in a genuine paradigm-shift scenario.

Sensitivity Analysis

The model's outputs are most sensitive to three parameters. The following table shows how the 2035 Debt/GDP changes under variations in each, holding all others at SPICE base case (40% displacement, no policy response):

Parameter variedLowBaseHighImpact on Debt/GDP 2035
AI displacement rate 5% (CBO) 40% (SPICE) 60% (Tsunami) ~135% → ~220% → >280%

Conclusion

The model is most sensitive to the displacement rate (which you can explore directly with the slider) and moderately sensitive to interest rate feedback. It is relatively robust to the productivity–inflation pass-through assumption. The displacement lag is important for timing of the crisis but less so for its ultimate severity.
